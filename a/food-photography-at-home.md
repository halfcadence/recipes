You don't need a studio or a lighting kit — a window, a sheet of paper, and a camera you already own will out-shoot most restaurant phone snaps. Good food photography is almost entirely about **controlling one soft light source** and getting out of its way. Everything below assumes a single window and works the same on a mirrorless body, a DSLR, or a recent phone.

## Light

Use one window, and only the window. Kill every overhead and lamp in the room — mixing daylight with warm bulbs gives you two colours of light fighting on the plate, and no white balance fixes it. Daylight is the most flattering light there is for food; you just have to shape it.

**Soften it.** Direct sun through the glass is too hard — it blows out highlights and casts a black-edged shadow. Tape a sheet of tracing paper, a white shower curtain, or a thin white bedsheet over the window. That turns a small hard source into a large soft one, and large-and-soft is the whole game: gentle gradients, glossy-not-glaring highlights, shadows with soft edges.

**Shoot on an overcast day if you can.** The sky becomes one giant softbox and the diffusion is done for you. Midday direct sun is the worst case; a bright cloudy day is the best.

## Direction

Put the light to the **side or behind** the food, never in front. Front light (window behind you, pointing at the plate) flattens everything and kills texture. Side light rakes across the surface and reveals it — the crumb of a cake, the sheen on a sauce, the fluff of rice. Back light (window behind the dish, camera facing into it) is the restaurant-menu look: it lights up drinks, soups, and anything glossy or translucent from within, and throws a long shadow toward you that reads as depth.

Start with the light at roughly 45° behind the plate and rotate the setup — not the camera — until the highlights land where you want them.

## Fill

Side and back light leave the camera-side of the food in shadow. Prop a **sheet of white foam board, card, or even a plate** on the dark side to bounce light back and open the shadow up. Move it closer for a brighter, flatter fill; pull it away for more drama. This one piece of white card is the difference between "lit" and "professionally lit."

For a moodier shot, do the opposite — put a sheet of **black card** on the shadow side (negative fill) to deepen it. Dark, dramatic food photos are almost always back light plus negative fill.

## Angle

Match the angle to the dish:

- **Straight down (flat lay, 90°)** — for plates that read as a shape from above: pizzas, spreads, bowls of soup, a table of many dishes. Everything is in the same plane, so it's easy to keep sharp.
- **Three-quarter (45°)** — the everyday default; how you'd see the plate sitting down to eat. Good for most mains and desserts.
- **Straight on (0°, eye level)** — for anything with height and layers: a stacked burger, a slice of cake, a drink in a tall glass, a canelé. Shows the strata a top-down shot would hide.

A tall custard like a **purin** or a canelé wants the 45°-to-straight-on range so you see its height and the wobble; a flat tart or a bowl of curry wants the top-down.

## Camera settings

Shoot **RAW** if the camera offers it — it gives you the latitude to fix white balance and recover highlights later. Then:

- **Aperture** around f/4–f/5.6 for a single hero dish (enough blur to separate it from the background, enough depth that the whole dish stays sharp). Stop down to f/8+ for a flat lay so the far edge doesn't go soft.
- **ISO** as low as it'll go (100–400). Food doesn't move, so you can afford a slow shutter.
- **Shutter** — whatever balances the exposure. On a tripod even 1/15s is fine.
- **White balance** to a custom/daylight preset, or fix it in RAW against something you know is white (the plate, the card). Getting white *actually* white is what separates a clean shot from a yellow-tinged one.

A **tripod** matters more than any lens. It lets you drop the ISO, keep the aperture where you want it, and — most usefully — lock the frame so you can tweak the styling and the light while watching the exact composition update.

## Styling

- **Wipe the plate.** Every drip and fingerprint on the rim shows. Keep a damp cloth and a cotton swab on hand.
- **Shoot it fresh, or fake fresh.** Ice cream melts, herbs wilt, crema collapses, steam vanishes. Have the shot composed and the camera set *before* the food arrives, then plate and shoot fast. For steam, a hidden cotton ball microwaved damp behind the dish reads as heat.
- **Undersauce for the camera, add the hero drip live.** A flooded plate photographs muddy; sauce lightly, then add one deliberate pour or spoonful in frame.
- **Odd numbers and negative space.** Three canelés read better than four; a plate placed off-centre with room to breathe beats one crammed edge to edge. Leave empty space — it makes the food the subject.
- **Garnish for contrast, not decoration.** A green herb on a brown braise, a dusting of sugar on a dark cake, a single glossy berry — one point of contrast does more than a scatter.

## Editing

The goal is to make the photo look like the food did in person, not to invent a look. In any RAW editor:

1. **Fix white balance first** — everything else depends on it. Neutralise against the whitest thing in the frame.
2. **Set the exposure** so the brightest highlight is bright but not clipped.
3. **Lift shadows** a little to recover detail the fill card didn't reach.
4. **Nudge contrast and texture** up slightly — food benefits from a touch of crunch.
5. **Leave the colours honest.** Over-saturated food looks fake and, worse, unappetising. Pull *back* on saturation before you push it.

The whole pipeline — one diffused window, a white bounce card, the right angle, RAW, and an honest edit — costs nothing beyond what's already in the kitchen, and it's most of the distance to photos that look like the recipe tasted.
