A practical guide to which spirits work best in which baked goods, based on testing and common sense. The goal: pick the right bottle so the flavor complements rather than fights the other ingredients.

## What alcohol actually does in baking

1. **Flavor** — the residual aroma after most alcohol evaporates. After 60 min at 350°F+, only ~5% of the alcohol remains, but the volatile flavor compounds linger.
2. **Crust formation** — alcohol evaporates faster than water, drying the outer surface and helping create crisp shells (relevant in canelés, pie crusts).
3. **Gluten inhibition** — unlike water, alcohol doesn't contribute to gluten formation, keeping batters tender.
4. **Extraction** — alcohol dissolves flavor compounds that water can't (vanilla, spices, fruit essences).

## The spirits

### Dark rum
- **Flavor profile:** Molasses, caramel, tropical fruit, brown sugar.
- **Best with:** Vanilla, caramel, banana, coconut, plain custard.
- **The canelé classic.** Rum's molasses depth is what makes a traditional canelé taste like a canelé. It's warm and sweet-coded without being actually sweet.
- **Avoid with:** Chocolate (gets lost or clashes — brandy is better).

### Brandy / Cognac
- **Flavor profile:** Grape, dried fruit, warm spice, smooth, slightly sweet.
- **Best with:** Chocolate, stone fruit, nuts, rich custards, dark caramel.
- **The chocolate match.** Grape-based fruitiness complements cocoa like chocolate-covered cherries. It supports rather than competes.
- **Functionally identical** for baking purposes. Cognac is just brandy from a specific French region. Use whichever is cheaper.

### Bourbon / Whiskey
- **Flavor profile:** Corn sweetness, oak, vanilla, smoke, sometimes peat.
- **Best with:** Warm spices (nutmeg, cinnamon), pecans, maple, brown butter, caramel, eggnog.
- **The holiday spirit.** Bourbon's vanilla-oak character is perfect alongside warm baking spices. Think pecan pie, bread pudding, eggnog canelé.
- **Avoid with:** Dark chocolate (smokiness competes with cocoa bitterness — two strong flavors fighting).

### Orange liqueur (Cointreau, Grand Marnier)
- **Flavor profile:** Bright citrus, sweet, slightly floral.
- **Best with:** Chocolate (classic combo), almond, vanilla, berries, cream.
- **Note:** ~24% sugar by weight. If using at full measure, consider reducing recipe sugar by ~15-20g per 80g of liqueur used.
- **Grand Marnier** is cognac-based (adds warmth). **Cointreau** is neutral spirit-based (cleaner citrus).

### Amaretto
- **Flavor profile:** Almond, marzipan, vanilla, sweet.
- **Best with:** Stone fruit (peach, cherry), chocolate, vanilla, almond-based pastries.
- **Very sweet** (~25% sugar). Reduce recipe sugar accordingly.

### Kahlúa / Coffee liqueur
- **Flavor profile:** Coffee, chocolate, vanilla, sweet.
- **Best with:** Chocolate, tiramisu, coffee-flavored bakes.
- **Redundant in coffee canelés** — just use instant espresso powder for more control and less sugar.

## Pairing matrix

| Bake flavor | Best spirit | Runner-up | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vanilla / plain | Dark rum | Brandy | — |
| Chocolate | Brandy / cognac | Orange liqueur | Whiskey |
| Coffee | Dark rum | Brandy | — |
| Warm spices / eggnog | Bourbon | Brandy | — |
| Matcha / hojicha | Dark rum | Skip entirely | Whiskey |
| Fruit (citrus) | Cointreau | Brandy | Rum |
| Nut / almond | Amaretto | Bourbon | — |
| Caramel | Bourbon | Dark rum | — |

## Practical notes

- **Can't taste it after baking?** Normal. Most of the flavor is in the aroma, not the tongue. You'll notice it more when comparing a batch with rum vs without, side by side.
- **Can I just skip it?** Yes. Replace with the same weight of milk. You'll lose some flavor complexity and crust quality but it'll still work.
- **Cheap vs expensive?** Doesn't matter for baking. The heat destroys any subtlety that makes expensive bottles worth sipping. Use the $10-15 bottle.
- **How much?** Most canelé/custard recipes use 4-5% of total batter weight as spirit. This is enough for aroma without making it boozy.
