Notes on the one pan that gets used the most around here: the Field No. 8 cast iron skillet. What it is, why it's good, how to season and care for it, and which recipes on this site are sized for it.

## What it is

The Field No. 8 is a 10¼-inch (26cm) cast iron skillet with a cooking surface of about 8 inches across. Field Company casts it thinner and lighter than vintage-style modern cast iron (Lodge and the like), machines the cooking surface smooth, and finishes it with a light factory seasoning. The result is a pan that heats a touch faster, is easier to lift, and starts smoother than rougher sand-cast pans — closer to a vintage Griswold or Wagner in feel.

"No. 8" is the old foundry sizing number, not a measurement — it dates back to when pan sizes were keyed to wood-stove eye openings. A No. 8 lands at ~10¼ inches across the top.

## Why cast iron for baking

- **Heat retention.** Cast iron holds a lot of thermal mass, so it stays hot when food (or cold batter) hits it. That steady heat is what crisps the edges of a clafoutis or sears a steak crust.
- **Oven-to-table.** It goes from stovetop to a 500°F oven to the table without a second thought. Most baked goods that want a crisp bottom benefit from a preheated or at least oven-stable vessel.
- **Even surface heat.** Once it's up to temperature, the whole base radiates evenly — no hot spots dancing around like thin aluminum.
- **The edges.** For custard-batter desserts (clafoutis, far breton, Dutch baby), the buttered cast iron wall fries the batter where it climbs, giving a crisp, lacy edge you don't get in glass or ceramic.

## Seasoning and care

- **Seasoning is just polymerized oil.** Thin coats of fat baked past their smoke point bond to the iron and to each other, building a slick, rust-proof, mildly nonstick surface. It's not a coating you buy — it's a finish you grow.
- **To maintain it:** cook with some fat, and after washing, dry the pan fully and wipe a very thin film of neutral oil over the cooking surface while warm. Thin is the whole game — visible pooled oil bakes sticky and gummy.
- **Washing.** Warm water and a brush or non-scratch scrubber is fine. A little soap is fine on modern seasoning; it's polymerized, not water-soluble. Dry immediately and completely — standing water is what rusts iron, not soap.
- **Stuck-on bits:** simmer a little water in the pan to loosen them, or scrub with coarse salt as a gentle abrasive. Avoid steel wool unless you're stripping to re-season.
- **Re-seasoning:** if it looks dull, patchy, or food starts sticking, wipe on a paper-thin coat of oil and bake upside down at ~450–500°F for an hour, then cool in the oven. Repeat a few times to rebuild.

## The acid problem

This is the one real gotcha for dessert bakers. Acidic ingredients — and **cherries, citrus, tomatoes, wine** all qualify — react with the iron and can strip a young or thin seasoning, leaving a metallic taste and a patchy pan. On a well-established, thickly seasoned pan a short bake is usually fine. But for a long, wet, acidic bake like a cherry clafoutis on newer seasoning, the safer move is:

- Use **enameled cast iron** (a Dutch oven lid-flipped, a Le Creuset skillet) if you have it, or
- Use a **stoneware, ceramic, or glass dish** for the most acidic, longest bakes, or
- Accept that you'll likely **re-season** the Field after a cherry dessert — not the end of the world, just a wipe-and-bake.

If you do bake acidic fruit in the bare Field, get it out of the pan reasonably soon after baking rather than letting it sit and soak.

## Cast iron vs a cake tin

You can bake any of these skillet desserts in a cake tin instead — it's mostly a tradeoff of crust versus convenience.

**Cast iron gives you:** crisp, lacy edges and a browned bottom. A preheated, heat-retentive skillet fries the batter where it climbs the wall — that browned edge is much of the appeal of a clafoutis or far breton. It also goes stovetop-to-oven-to-table.

**A cake tin gives you:** easy release and serving (especially a springform or loose-bottom tin), no acid worry (coated aluminum, glass, or ceramic doesn't react with cherries the way bare cast iron does), and lighter handling. The cost is weaker edges — a thin tin heats slower and holds less heat, so the result is paler and more uniformly soft, more "baked custard" than "crisp-edged."

**Which recipe cares:** the clafoutis and sour cream clafoutis lean on those crisp edges, so they lose the most in a tin (still good, just softer). The far breton cares least — it's dense and sliceable by design, so a clean springform release is arguably a win.

**If you use a tin:** butter it well (or line the base with parchment for a non-reactive tin), preheat a baking sheet and set the tin on it for a better bottom, and add a few minutes since it heats slower than preheated cast iron. A ~9-inch round is close to the Field No. 8's capacity.

## Recipes sized for the Field No. 8

The 10¼-inch Field holds roughly **600–660g of batter** at a good clafoutis depth. A few recipes here are written or scaled to that:

- [Cherry Clafoutis]({{ '/r/cherry-clafoutis' | relative_url }}) — sized directly for the Field No. 8 (~656g batter). The reference bake.
- [Sour Cream Cherry Clafoutis]({{ '/r/sour-cream-cherry-clafoutis' | relative_url }}) — the base is for a small dish; the notes include a 2× version that fills the Field No. 8.
- [Cherry Far Breton]({{ '/r/cherry-far-breton' | relative_url }}) — the dense, sliceable cousin; bakes happily in the skillet too (use enameled or stoneware if your seasoning is young, since it's also cherry-and-rum acidic).

As a rule of thumb, any custard-batter or skillet dessert written for a "9–10 inch dish" or "9-inch skillet" will scale to the Field No. 8 at roughly 1.2–1.3× — or just fill it to the same depth and adjust the bake time up a few minutes for the extra batter.
