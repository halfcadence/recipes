Single-serve and pair packaging for canelés: waxed, glassine, and grease-resistant kraft bags, ranked by value and fit. Companion to [Packing Canelés](./packing-caneles), which covers boxes. Sizes assume a 55mm mold: a baked canelé ~55–60mm across the top (with liner), ~50mm tall.

## What these bags are

Three surfaces solve the same problem differently. **Glassine** is paper calendered (pressure-rolled) until it's smooth and semi-translucent — naturally grease- and moisture-resistant with no coating, the signature bakery-counter look. **Grease-resistant kraft** is natural-brown or white paper treated (Quilon/silicone-type) to hold a grease barrier while staying matte and porous. **Waxed** is true wax-coated paper — the strongest grease barrier and a classic look, but the slickest surface of all. The functional point for canelés is the same across all three: **paper breathes**, so the lacquered shell stays crisp, where a sealed plastic bag steams it soft within hours. This is the same rule from the box guide — eat the same day, gift fresh, and say so — a breathable bag buys you the same few hours a window box does, no more.

## Per-bag economics

Cheapest viable per-bag is **~$0.03** — Uline glassine or Epakh kraft at carton/500-count volume. At realistic small-shop pack sizes (100ct), figure **~$0.08–0.13** for a flat bag. A *stamped* bag costs essentially the same: pigment-pad ink amortizes to roughly **$0 per impression** (one craft pad does thousands), so a stamped kraft 4×6" lands at **~$0.10–0.13 all-in** — call it a dime. That's well under the box options in [Packing Canelés](./packing-caneles): a single kraft window box runs more in materials and footprint, where a bag is one cheap sheet of paper. Against the product itself it's noise — a canelé costs well under a dollar to make and sells for several. A $0.03–$0.10 bag is **under 3% of a $4 price**. Packaging is not where the money is; pick for fit and look, not to save pennies.

## Recommendation

- **Pop-up / market stall (cheap, stamp a logo):** natural **kraft grease-resistant 4×6"**. It breathes, takes a 2-color stamp beautifully, and reads as intentional craft. Stamp the bag face directly. For a **pair**, step up to a **5.7×6.5" kraft** (fits two side-by-side) or a small gusseted bag so they stand and don't squash.
- **Gift (window / nicer):** a **self-sealing kraft window bag** (shows the dark shell) or **flat glassine** for the translucent patisserie look. Fold the top — do not seal the adhesive strip airtight, or the shell softens. Put the branding on a kraft band, hangtag, or sticker rather than the slick face.
- **Sizing:** **3×5"** or **4×6"** holds **one**. For **two**, use **5×7"-ish (5.7×6.5")** flat or a **gusseted bag** — two in a 4×6" is a tight squeeze that risks marring the ridges.

## Honest flags

- **Confidence:** High on the glassine breathe/grease behavior, the Uline FDA/USDA lines, and the stamp ranking (kraft > white > glassine > wax). Medium on several Amazon per-bag prices and pack counts — listings and packs change, so verify dimensions and price before ordering. Treat product names as starting points, not endorsements.
- **Glassine smears ink — the main gotcha.** The calendered surface is non-absorbent; standard water/dye stamp-pad ink beads, smears, and rubs off. If you must stamp glassine, use a pigment or solvent (StazOn-type) pad and heat-set, or accept smudging — easier to stamp a separate kraft band or sticker. **Waxed paper is worse:** wax repels water-based ink entirely and can crack/flake.
- **Food-contact:** use a food-safe pigment pad, stamp the outside only, keep ink off where it touches the pastry, and let stamped bags cure ~24h before use. Test-stamp each lot — coatings vary between manufacturers.
- **Never seal airtight.** Self-seal window bags and heat-sealable kraft will steam the shell soft if fully closed — fold the top instead.
