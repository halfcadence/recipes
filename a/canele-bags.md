Single-serve and pair packaging for canelés: waxed, glassine, and grease-resistant kraft bags, ranked by value and fit. Companion to [Packing Canelés](./packing-caneles), which covers boxes. Sizes assume a 55mm mold: a baked canelé ~55–60mm across the top (with liner), ~50mm tall.

## What these bags are

Three surfaces solve the same problem differently. **Glassine** is paper calendered (pressure-rolled) until it's smooth and semi-translucent — naturally grease- and moisture-resistant with no coating, the signature bakery-counter look. **Grease-resistant kraft** is natural-brown or white paper treated (Quilon/silicone-type) to hold a grease barrier while staying matte and porous. **Waxed** is true wax-coated paper — the strongest grease barrier and a classic look, but the slickest surface of all. The functional point for canelés is the same across all three: **paper breathes**, so the lacquered shell stays crisp, where a sealed plastic bag steams it soft within hours. This is the same rule from the box guide — eat the same day, gift fresh, and say so — a breathable bag buys you the same few hours a window box does, no more.

## Per-bag economics

Cheapest viable per-bag is **~$0.03** — Uline glassine or Epakh kraft at carton/500-count volume. At realistic small-shop pack sizes (100ct), figure **~$0.08–0.13** for a flat bag. A *stamped* bag costs essentially the same: pigment-pad ink amortizes to roughly **$0 per impression** (one craft pad does thousands), so a stamped kraft 4×6" lands at **~$0.10–0.13 all-in** — call it a dime. That's well under the box options in [Packing Canelés](./packing-caneles): a single kraft window box runs more in materials and footprint, where a bag is one cheap sheet of paper. Against the product itself it's noise — a canelé costs well under a dollar to make and sells for several. A $0.03–$0.10 bag is **under 3% of a $4 price**. Packaging is not where the money is; pick for fit and look, not to save pennies.

## Recommendation

- **Pop-up / market stall (cheap, stamp a logo):** natural **kraft grease-resistant 4×6"**. It breathes, takes a 2-color stamp beautifully, and reads as intentional craft. Stamp the bag face directly. For a **pair**, step up to a **5.7×6.5" kraft** (fits two side-by-side) or a small gusseted bag so they stand and don't squash.
- **Gift (window / nicer):** a **self-sealing kraft window bag** (shows the dark shell) or **flat glassine** for the translucent patisserie look. Fold the top — do not seal the adhesive strip airtight, or the shell softens. Put the branding on a kraft band, hangtag, or sticker rather than the slick face.
- **Sizing:** **3×5"** or **4×6"** holds **one**. For **two**, use **5×7"-ish (5.7×6.5")** flat or a **gusseted bag** — two in a 4×6" is a tight squeeze that risks marring the ridges.

## Honest flags

- **Confidence:** High on the glassine breathe/grease behavior, the Uline FDA/USDA lines, and the stamp ranking (kraft > white > glassine > wax). Medium on several Amazon per-bag prices and pack counts — listings and packs change, so verify dimensions and price before ordering. Treat product names as starting points, not endorsements.
- **Glassine smears ink — the main gotcha.** The calendered surface is non-absorbent; standard water/dye stamp-pad ink beads, smears, and rubs off. If you must stamp glassine, use a pigment or solvent (StazOn-type) pad and heat-set, or accept smudging — easier to stamp a separate kraft band or sticker. **Waxed paper is worse:** wax repels water-based ink entirely and can crack/flake.
- **Food-contact:** use a food-safe pigment pad, stamp the outside only, keep ink off where it touches the pastry, and let stamped bags cure ~24h before use. Test-stamp each lot — coatings vary between manufacturers.
- **Never seal airtight.** Self-seal window bags and heat-sealable kraft will steam the shell soft if fully closed — fold the top instead.

## Stand-up & flat-bottom bags

Everything above lies flat. Two folds make a bag *stand* on a stall table — and a canelé is squat and **heavy for its size** (~55–60mm base, ~50mm tall, dense custardy crumb), so that weight is what decides which fold actually holds. **Flat-bottom / SOS (block-bottom):** the base glues open into a rectangular footprint and the walls become a box-like prism. This is the real stander — the glued base plus side gussets give it a footing, and the canelé's low weight keeps it planted. **Roll-top triangle:** a plain flat bag (a French-fry / glassine-style sleeve) you fill, then roll the top down so the mouth pinches into a triangular prism. It leans more than it stands, but it's the cheapest fold and reads artisanal. Each beats a flat sleeve when you need a bag to stand on its own, hold a pair upright without squashing, or carry more gift presence. Same rules carry over: **matte kraft takes a stamp, glassine doesn't, and never seal it airtight** — fold loosely so the shell stays crisp.

One sizing rule for the stand-up family: read the **base/gusset** dimension (the middle number), not the flat width. You need ~2.5"+ of base for **one** canelé to sit flat, ~3" for a comfortable single, and ~3.25"+ to seat **two** side-by-side.

### Closures

Three closures, matched to the shapes. **Fold-down top + sticker** is the default for the SOS popcorn and cookie bags: fold the top 2–3 times, seal with a round kraft sticker. It holds the box shape, gives a clean branded face, costs the least, and isn't airtight, so the shell breathes. **Tin-tie** (built into the bakery and coffee bags) is a fold-over metal strip that reseals without a sticker, reads "bakery," and reopens cleanly; fold it loosely and it breathes fine. **Rolled / crimped top** is the triangle fold itself — very breathable (the fold's open ends pass air), looks handmade, but doesn't seal and barely free-stands. All three beat a heat-seal or zip pouch, which traps steam and weeps butter. The sweet spot for a buttery canelé held a few hours: **grease-resistant (EcoCraft) but *un*-poly-lined kraft, folded loosely** — best grease barrier that still breathes. Plain kraft breathes best but resists grease least; poly-lined resists grease best but breathes least.

**Grease is the deciding factor at small scale:** plain uncoated kraft spots through if the canelé is even slightly warm. Pick a glassine-lined or poly-lined bag, or insert a glassine/parchment liner. A sensible first order: one 100-ct glassine-lined roll-top + one 50-ct flat-bottom window bag, then test-fit a real canelé and check for grease bleed before committing.

### More honest flags

- **Confidence:** High on shape behavior (flat-bottom stands, roll-top leans), the base-dimension sizing rule, and the WebstaurantStore specs/per-bag math at the listed pack counts. Medium on the *exact* two-canelé fit — "side-by-side" assumes the 55–60mm base; verify with one bag before committing to a case.

## Boxes & the elevated tier

Bags are for grab-and-go: cheap, flat-packing, stamped, done. Boxes and elevated packaging are for the other jobs — gifts, holidays, and the "drop" photo where presentation is the product. The honest tradeoff: every step up costs more per unit, takes more storage volume flat or assembled, and adds packing time. None of that means a bag is "basic and sad" — and if it reads that way, the fix is cheap (a sticker or belly-band, below), not a custom box order.

Two canelé constraints carry across every option here:

1. **Grease** — nest each canelé in a greaseproof/glassine liner, every box, no exceptions.
2. **Don't seal airtight** — pick tuck, slide, magnetic, or window closures, never heat-seal or gasket. A trapped canelé goes soft. Note "best same day" on the card.

### Custom-printed boxes — the honest reality

True custom boxes are real, but premature for a pop-up. The economics:

| Vendor | Box MOQ | Per-unit (small run) | Lead time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Arka | 10–25 | mailers from ~$0.99; tuck boxes higher | ~1 week |
| Packlane | 25 (paperboard cartons) | ~$2–5, drops with volume; 48-hr rush option | ~2–3 weeks |
| noissue | ~100+ (boxes) | only attractive at a few hundred | ~3 weeks |
| BRP / PrintNinja (offset) | 250–500+ | cheapest at 1,000+, plus plate/setup fees | longest |

The color catch: low-MOQ vendors print digital CMYK, not spot ink — and the brand's fluoro-pink (`#ff48b0`) is exactly the near-neon CMYK can't hit, so it prints duller. Faithful two-ink reproduction needs spot/Pantone offset, which means 500–1,000+ MOQ and plate fees.

**Verdict:** defer. Custom boxes are a phase-2 move (café / steady wholesale). When volume is predictable and a box size has settled, test one workhorse SKU (likely the 4-count) on Packlane or Arka first; reserve spot/Pantone offset for 500–1,000+ runs.

### The middle path (recommended)

Buy a nice stock box, brand it with a custom riso sticker, belly-band, or stamp. This is the sweet spot, and it's the workflow already started.

Why it wins:

- **Gift-grade look** for cash-light money — roughly $0.20–0.60 per box of branding on top of the stock box.
- **Low MOQ.** Sticker Mule does die-cut/circle stickers from 10 units (~$1.20/ea at 50, ~$0.48/ea at 200, ~$0.23/ea at 1,000); free proof, ~4-day turnaround.
- **Better color than a box.** A sticker prints as its own full-color object, so it reproduces the Blue (`#0078bf`) + Fluoro-Pink (`#ff48b0`) palette far more faithfully than CMYK box printing — and pops against cream/kraft exactly as the riso look intends.
- **On-brand with the stamp system.** Stamp the utility pieces (bags, liners, box interior) with the one-ink wordmark/glyph; sticker the hero pieces (lid seal, belly-band) with the two-color mark. A true two-color stamp fights registration — let the sticker carry the second ink.
- **Airtight-safe.** A sticker lid-seal or paper belly-band is a peel/tear closure: holds the box shut, brands it, traps no moisture.
- **Scales.** The sticker artwork is the same print file you hand a custom-box vendor later. Nothing is wasted moving pop-up → café.

Concrete recipe: kraft ballotin or 4-count window box + one greaseproof liner per canelé + a die-cut Sticker Mule lid seal (two-color glyph/wordmark) *or* a printed/stamped paper belly-band + the rubber stamp on the bag + a "best same day" card. Sticker vendors: Sticker Mule (MOQ 10, lowest entry), StickerApp (low-MOQ, Pantone/eco), noissue (sheets from ~250, rolls from ~1,000 — plus custom tissue to wrap a piece inside any plain box). Lowest-volume start: blank Avery round labels (90/180/450 packs) printed at home — fidelity limited by your printer, fine for a farmers-market table.
