The custardy [Canelé](/r/canele) built around a strong steep of Thai tea mix — that malty Assam black tea with its vanilla note and signature orange. Alcohol-free; the tea blend carries its own aromatics, reinforced here with real vanilla. A clean, from-the-bag rendering of cha yen in a caramelized shell.

## Ingredients

| | |
|---|---|
| 440g | milk |
| 28g | Thai tea mix (ChaTraMue "Number One" brand) |
| 38g | evaporated milk (or heavy cream) |
| 45g | butter |
| 1 | vanilla bean (or 8g vanilla extract) |
| 170g | granulated sugar |
| 2g | fleur de sel |
| 1 | whole egg (~50g) |
| 2 | egg yolks (~36g) |
| 40g | milk (added at the end, in place of the rum) |
| 85g | AP flour |
| 15g | cornstarch |
| | Nonstick spray for molds |
| | *Optional:* 10g sweetened condensed milk (folded in with the sugar), and/or a few drops orange gel dye |

## Steps

1. Heat the milk to 175°F — do not boil. Off heat, add the Thai tea mix, cover, and steep 12–15 minutes. Steep harder than you think you need to: the flavor mutes sharply over a 60-minute bake, so this is a deliberately strong infusion (~7–8% mix-to-milk, well above drink strength).

2. Strain through a fine sieve lined with cheesecloth or a cloth tea filter — the mix is dust-fine and a plain strainer lets the fines through; pass it through 2–3 times for a clean, deep-orange liquid. **Weigh the strained milk and measure out 340g** — top up with fresh milk if short (the recipe starts with extra to cover absorption). Return the 340g to the pot with the evaporated milk, butter, and vanilla and warm gently to melt the butter.

3. In a bowl, whisk sugar, salt, eggs, and yolks together (fold in the optional 10g condensed milk here).

4. Slowly pour about ¼ of the warm infused milk into the egg mixture, whisking to temper. Sift in flour and cornstarch, whisk until smooth.

5. Gradually add the remaining milk, whisking until smooth. Stir in the 40g milk.

6. Strain through a sieve. Refrigerate overnight (24 hours minimum).

7. Coat molds with nonstick spray. Stir rested batter gently. Preheat oven to 525°F. Fill each mold with 70g.

8. Bake at 525°F for 11 minutes, then drop to 375°F and bake 50–55 minutes more.

9. Unmold immediately onto a cooling rack. Cool 2 hours. Eat the same day.

## Notes

- **Use real Thai tea mix** — ChaTraMue "Number One Brand" (the orange one) — not plain black tea. The blend is Assam ("red tea") dust + sugar + artificial (vanilla-forward) flavor + FD&C Yellow #6 dye. The malty tea, the vanilla note, and the signature orange all come from the mix; plain black tea tastes wrong and bakes flat brown.
- **Infusion strength is the whole game.** Dessert recipes that under-steep come out bland (the Basque-cheesecake bakers warn a weaker tea "results in a bland cheesecake" at ~6% mix-to-dairy with a 5-minute steep). A canelé bakes long and hot, which is the worst case for aromatic retention, so this pushes higher — ~28g into the milk (~7–8%) with a longer steep. If a test batch reads faint, bump to 32–34g or steep longer rather than reaching for more dye. Infusing into milk/dairy (not water) matters: fat carries the aromatics through the oven better than water does.
- **Color mutes under the shell.** Two ways: the orange dilutes to a pale tan the moment it hits the batter, and it dulls further toward beige under high-heat Maillard browning. Expect an orange-gold interior under a dark shell, not neon. If you want the dessert to *read* "Thai tea" before the first bite, a few drops of orange gel dye in the batter is standard practice — flavor (from a strong infusion) and color (from supplemental dye) have to be reinforced separately.
- **Condensed/evaporated milk = authentic richness.** The drink's signature is two dairies: condensed milk for sweetness + body + dairy-caramel, evaporated milk for lighter creaminess. This uses evaporated milk in place of the cream as the infusion vehicle, and an optional 10g condensed milk in the batter; a thin drizzle of condensed milk on the unmolded canelé is the closest thing to the iced-tea ribbon.
- **No alcohol.** The original rum (40g) is replaced gram-for-gram with milk to hold the liquid balance, plus the vanilla bean to backfill the volatile note that bakes off. For the boozy, warm-spiced counterpart, see the [Spiced Thai Tea Canelé](/r/spiced-thai-tea-canele) — it restores the rum and adds chai-style spice; this version stays clean, sellable with zero alcohol disclosure (see [`../cafe/alcohol-in-pastry.md`](../cafe/alcohol-in-pastry.md)), and is one of the cheapest, most reliable Lane-B rotating teas (see [`../cafe/flavor-strategy.md`](../cafe/flavor-strategy.md)).
- Milk starts at 440g (vs the base 340g) to cover what the (now larger) charge of mix soaks up. Spent tea retains roughly 2–4× its dry weight; the fine-cut Thai mix lands lower-to-middle per gram, so 28g takes up somewhere near 60–110g. The exact figure doesn't matter — after steeping you weigh the strained milk and measure back to a clean 340g, so absorption can't dilute the batter.
- **Baking steel option:** with a preheated baking steel (tray on the steel), the Perfect-Loaf-style **450°F for 15 min → 350°F for 50–60 min** is a viable alternative to the 525→375 profile — see [`../cafe/baking-steel-notes.md`](../cafe/baking-steel-notes.md) and [`../cafe/experiment-start-temp.md`](../cafe/experiment-start-temp.md).
- Other technique is identical to the custardy [Canelé](/r/canele).

**Custardy-Canelé ratios (~12% structure)** — the Thai tea mix steeps in as flavor and color only; measuring the strained milk back to 340g keeps the liquid fraction identical to the base [Canelé](/r/canele), and replacing the rum with milk leaves it unchanged. The only lever moved versus the existing Thai tea recipe is a stronger infusion (28g vs 20g mix), which lives entirely in the steep-and-strain step and never touches structure.
