Canelés salés — the apéritif version. The custard base with the sugar stripped out, loaded with bacon, Comté, and caramelized onion (the Cake d'Alsace trio). Crisp shell, savory custard inside; served warm as a bite, not a dessert.

## Ingredients

| | |
|---|---|
| | **Batter** |
| 340g | milk |
| 45g | butter |
| 10g | granulated sugar (for shell color only) |
| 4g | fine salt |
| 1g | black pepper |
| | pinch of nutmeg |
| 2 | whole eggs (~110g) |
| 1 | egg yolk (~18g) |
| 100g | AP flour |
| | **Mix-ins** |
| 80g | bacon, cut into lardons, cooked and drained |
| 80g | Comté or Gruyère, grated |
| 60g | caramelized onion (from ~1 onion) |
| 8g | chives or parsley, chopped |
| | Butter or nonstick spray for molds |

## Steps

1. Cook the bacon lardons until golden, drain on paper towel. In the same pan, caramelize the sliced onion low and slow until deep golden, ~15 minutes. Cool both.

2. In a pot, heat milk and butter to 175°F — do not boil. Remove from heat.

3. Whisk the sugar, salt, pepper, nutmeg, eggs, and yolk together.

4. Pour about ¼ of the hot milk into the eggs, whisking to temper. Sift in the flour, whisk smooth. Add the remaining milk, whisk until combined.

5. Strain through a sieve. Refrigerate the base batter at least 2 hours (overnight is fine).

6. Stir the bacon, cheese, caramelized onion, and herbs into the rested batter.

7. Butter the molds. Preheat oven to 475°F. Fill each mold ~80% (the mix-ins make it thicker than sweet batter).

8. Bake at 475°F for 11 minutes, then drop to 375°F and bake 45–50 minutes more, until set and the shells are deep golden.

9. Unmold onto a rack. Serve warm — they're best within an hour or two, as an apéro bite.

<div class="notes" markdown="1">
## Notes

- Savory canelés trade the dessert's caramelized sugar shell for a cheese-and-fat-browned one — expect a paler, softer shell than the dark sweet version, since there's almost no sugar to caramelize. The 10g sugar is just for a little color and browning.
- The cheese and bacon both bring salt, so taste before adding much more — 4g added salt assumes unsalted-ish bacon; cut to 2g if your bacon is very salty.
- Mix-ins are folded in *after* the rest, not before, so they don't sink or stew in the batter overnight.
- Swap freely within the meat + cheese + veg framework: ham or pancetta for bacon; gruyère, cheddar, or parmesan for Comté; sautéed leek, sun-dried tomato, or roasted pepper for the onion. Keep total mix-ins around 220g so the batter still binds.
- Best eaten warm and fresh — savory canelés soften faster than sweet ones and aren't as good the next day.
- No beeswax needed; butter the molds (it adds savory flavor) or use nonstick spray.
- Eat warm with an apéritif. A 55mm mold gives a substantial bite; smaller apéro molds make one- or two-bite versions.
</div>

## Analysis

<div class="analysis" markdown="1">

Total batter weight: **~855g** (batter + mix-ins; excludes mold coating)

| Component | Amount | % |
|---|---|---|
| Structure solids (flour) | 100g | 11.7% |
| Fat (butter, cheese, bacon, egg, milk) | 120g | 14.0% |
| Sugar (token + lactose + onion) | 30g | 3.5% |
| Liquid (milk, egg water, meat/veg moisture) | 535g | 62.6% |
| Egg/cheese/meat protein | 71g | 8.3% |

**The mirror image of a sweet canelé.** Sugar collapses from ~23% to ~3.5% (just a touch for shell color), while fat and protein jump — the cheese and bacon pile on both. Structure stays ~12% (flour-set custard), but with so little sugar the proportion that's liquid rises. The big shift is **protein at ~8%** (the highest of any canelé here), from cheese and bacon, which is what makes it set savory-custardy and brown without caramel.

</div>
