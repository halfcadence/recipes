Adapted from Yoshida Morihide's banana bread — a baking-soda-and-powder loaf with ~36% ripe banana, loosened with a little oil and cream for a moist, open crumb. Use the ripest, most black-spotted fruit you have. Warm from the oven it's fragrant and fluffy; the next day it settles moist and cohesive.

## Ingredients

| | |
|---|---|
| 50g | unsalted butter, softened |
| 2g | salt |
| 86g | light brown sugar (for cassonade) |
| 105g | whole egg (~2 large) |
| 96g | cake flour |
| 96g | all-purpose flour |
| 4g | baking soda |
| 4g | baking powder |
| 43g | honey |
| 340g | very ripe banana (black-spotted), mashed |
| 30g | neutral oil |
| 40g | heavy cream (or milk) |
| 40g | walnuts, chopped (optionally toasted) |

## Steps

1. Butter a 6-inch round cake pan (or a 9×5 loaf) and line with parchment. Preheat the oven to 340°F (or start at 350°F to set the top, then drop to 325°F after 15–20 minutes — see notes).

2. Cream the softened butter, salt, and brown sugar 2–3 minutes until light.

3. Beat in the egg gradually, then the honey. Whisk in the oil and cream until smooth. If it looks split, warm the bowl briefly or add a spoonful of the flour.

4. Whisk the cake flour, all-purpose flour, baking soda, and baking powder together. Fold into the batter along with the mashed banana, mixing just until no dry streaks remain — don't overwork it.

5. Fold in the walnuts last.

6. Scrape into the pan. Bake at 340°F until a skewer in the center comes out with a few moist crumbs (no wet batter) — about 55–70 minutes for a deep 6-inch, 45–55 for a loaf. Tent with foil around 35–40 minutes if the top browns before the center sets. The center should read 205–208°F internally if you have a thermometer.

7. Cool in the pan 10 minutes, then turn out. Best the next day; eat within 1–2 days.

Total batter weight: **936g** (banana is ~36% of it)

| Component | Amount | % |
|---|---|---|
| Structure solids (flour + fruit/nut fiber) | 206g | 22.0% |
| Fat (butter, oil, cream, egg, walnut) | 121g | 12.9% |
| Sugar (brown sugar, honey, banana) | 191g | 20.4% |
| Liquid (banana, egg, cream, honey water) | 383g | 40.9% |
| Egg/nut/fruit/dairy protein | 24g | 2.6% |

**A quick bread loosened toward a lighter crumb.** Banana still supplies most of the liquid and much of the sugar with no added milk, but a shot of oil and cream push fat to ~13% (from ~9% in the plain original), and baking powder joins the soda for extra lift — so the crumb opens up and eats moister. Structure stays high for a cake (~22%) because it still needs enough gluten to hold the heavy wet fruit without sinking, which is why the flour is a deliberate blend of cake (tender) and all-purpose (structure) rather than all of one.

## Notes

- **Ripeness is everything.** Use heavily black-spotted to nearly-black bananas — riper means sweeter, softer, and far more banana aroma, and the acid is what drives the baking soda. Underripe banana gives a bland, pale, under-risen loaf. Not there yet? See [Ripening Bananas](/a/ripening-bananas) — and note the oven/microwave shortcuts soften without really sweetening, so nudge the sugar up if you use them.
- **Cassonade → light brown sugar.** The original calls for cassonade (French unrefined cane sugar); light brown sugar is the clean 1:1 sub for its caramel/molasses note and moisture. Turbinado or demerara is more flavor-accurate but coarser — pulse it fine or it can stay gritty.
- **Oil and cream loosen the crumb.** The plain Yoshida formula is lean on fat (~9%) and eats like a moist pound cake; the 30g oil and 40g cream here lift fat to ~13% and open it up. Oil stays liquid at room temperature so it reads looser and moister than butter alone; the cream adds fat, moisture, and a little richness. Milk subs for the cream for a lighter, less-rich loaf. Want it looser still? Melt the butter and just whisk everything (muffin method) instead of creaming.
- **The two-flour blend is deliberate.** The original pairs a weak flour (薄力粉, cake) with a "medium-strong" flour (中強力粉, ~10.5–11.5% protein) — cake flour alone would be too fragile to carry 340g of wet banana and would gum in the center; all bread flour would go tough. All-purpose is the clean stand-in for the medium-strong half, giving an effective ~9.5–10% protein: tender but sturdy. In a pinch, all-AP (192g) works fine — this is a forgiving quick bread.
- **Soda and baking powder together.** The soda needs the banana, honey, and brown sugar's acid to fire, and it browns the crust; the baking powder adds the extra lift that opens the crumb. Whisk both evenly into the flour — a stray clump of soda leaves a soapy, yellowish spot. That's the only real risk, and it's low.
- **No 340°F on your oven?** 340°F is just "between 325 and 350." For a deep pan, either bake straight at **325°F** (add ~5–10 min, gentlest on the center) or **start at 350°F for 15–20 minutes** to set the domed top, then **drop to 325°F** for the rest — the "start hot, finish gentle" route gives good lift and a fully-cooked middle. Plain 350°F works too but browns fast, so tent earlier (~30 min) and watch the center.
- **Original vs our deep-pan bake.** Yoshida bakes the plain version at **180°C (356°F) for 35 min**, splitting the batter between **two small shallow bread pans** — split into two shallow masses, the centers heat fast, so a short, hot bake works. Baked as **one tall column in a single deep 6×3-inch round**, the center sits far from every surface, and 180°C would scorch the dome before the middle set — so drop the oven ~25°F and roughly double the time, as above. Two small loaf pans? Use his original 180°C / 35 min.
- **Scaling.** The full recipe (~936g) fills a **9×5 loaf** or a **deep 6×3-inch round** (bakes tall — tent the top and give it the longer time). For a standard **6×2-inch** round, scale to ~0.71× (butter 36g, brown sugar 61g, egg 75g, cake + AP 68g each, soda 3g, baking powder 3g, honey 31g, banana 241g, oil 21g, cream 28g, walnuts 28g) and bake ~40–50 min.
- **Serving it as a cake.** Lovely warm with lightly whipped or brown-butter [whipped cream](/r/whipped-cream), a spoon of [salted caramel](/r/salted-caramel-sauce), or vanilla/salted-caramel ice cream; caramelized banana and toasted walnuts on top push it fully into dessert.
- **Keeps a day or two.** The oil and cream help it hold moisture a touch longer than an all-butter loaf, but ~36% banana still means it's best day-of to next-day. Freezes well wrapped tight if you want to hold it longer.
</div>
