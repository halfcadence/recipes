Whipped in a tall deli container with an immersion blender — the fastest hand method there is. It reaches soft peaks in under a minute, so watch the cream, not the clock.

## Ingredients

| | |
|---|---|
| 240g | heavy cream (36% fat), cold |
| 14g | confectioners' sugar |
| 2g | vanilla extract (~½ tsp) |
| | pinch of salt (optional) |

## Steps

1. Freeze a tall, narrow pint deli container (or wide-mouth jar) and the immersion blender's head for 10–15 minutes. Keep the cream in the fridge until the moment you use it.

2. Pour the cold cream, confectioners' sugar, vanilla, and salt into the chilled container. It must be no more than **half full** — the cream nearly doubles.

3. Submerge the blender head all the way to the bottom, tilted slightly to one side. Blend on medium (or pulse). Hold it at the bottom 10–15 seconds to start a vortex, then slowly lift toward — not through — the surface and dive back down. Keep the blade submerged the whole time.

4. Watch the cream, not a timer. It goes from pourable to soft peaks in well under a minute and to stiff peaks seconds after that. Stop and check often: lift the head out and read the peak.

5. Stop at the stage you want (see notes). Serve, or cover and refrigerate.

## Notes

- **The three stages — lift the blade out and read the peak.** *Soft:* the peak flops over immediately; loose and airy — for folding into mousses and batters. *Medium:* the peak stands but its tip curls over; billowy and smooth — the all-purpose stage for topping and dolloping. *Stiff:* the peak stands straight and holds, still glossy — for piping and stabilized fillings. Stop the instant it turns matte or grainy.
- **Keep the blade submerged.** There's no lid — a blade breaking the surface splatters, and a blade spinning in air doesn't aerate. Never pull the running head out of the cream.
- **Tall and narrow beats a wide bowl.** The narrow container forces the cream through the blade guard and builds a fast vortex; a wide bowl just splashes it around without whipping. Fill no more than halfway.
- **Blade vs. whisk attachment.** If your blender came with a whisk attachment, use it — it's gentler and more forgiving. The plain blade works (it's what most people use here) but aerates faster, so it over-whips a few seconds sooner. Either way, medium speed or pulsing gives the most control.
- **Cold is everything.** The set is a scaffold of solid butterfat holding air; warm fat turns liquid and collapses the bubbles. Cream straight from the fridge (≤40°F) whips fastest and stiffest — room-temperature cream may never reach stiff peaks. On a hot day, set the container in an ice bath.
- **Fat content.** Needs at least ~30% fat to whip; 36% heavy/heavy whipping cream is ideal and the most forgiving with the blender's speed. Half-and-half and light cream won't whip.
- **Sugar.** Confectioners' is the default — it dissolves instantly and the cornstarch in it adds a little stability; granulated works but can leave a slight grittiness. About 14g (2 Tbsp) per 240g cream is lightly sweet; use up to ~28g for dessert-sweet. Add it at the start with this method — it's too fast to pause and add mid-whip. Salt is optional and divisive; a tiny pinch rounds out the sweetness, more than that reads salty.
- **Rescue over-whipped cream.** If it's grainy but not yet broken, whisk in a splash of cold unwhipped cream by hand — 1–2 Tbsp for a slight over-whip, up to ¼ of the original volume if it's worse — to rebuild the emulsion. Once it separates into butter and buttermilk, it's done; start over (or finish making butter).
- **Storage.** Plain whipped cream holds a few hours to about a day before it weeps and deflates; keep it airtight and don't stir it before serving. To hold it longer, whip in a stabilizer: a spoonful of mascarpone or ~½–1 tsp bloomed gelatin per 240g cream (48h–3 days), or confectioners' sugar alone (~12h, the zero-effort option). For weepy leftovers, set the cream in a fine-mesh strainer over a bowl in the fridge — the liquid drains and the cream stays fluffy.
- **Scaling.** A pint deli container holds ~240g cream at the half-full line; step up to a quart container for ~480g. Don't overfill — headroom for expansion is what keeps it from climbing the blender and splattering.
