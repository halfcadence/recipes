Cold cream beaten to the peak you want. A hand mixer is the everyday tool — slow enough to be forgiving, fast enough to be quick. Watch the cream, not the clock: near the end it goes from perfect to over-whipped in seconds.

## Ingredients

| | |
|---|---|
| 240g | heavy cream (36% fat), cold |
| 14g | confectioners' sugar |
| 2g | vanilla extract (~½ tsp) |
| | pinch of salt (optional) |

## Steps

1. Chill a deep bowl (metal is best) and the beaters in the freezer 15–20 minutes (or the fridge ~30 minutes). Keep the cream in the fridge until the moment you use it.

2. Combine the cold cream, confectioners' sugar, vanilla, and salt in the chilled bowl. (Or hold the sugar and add it once the cream thickens — see notes.)

3. Beat on **low** for ~30 seconds, until frothy with small bubbles. Starting low keeps thin cream from splattering out of the bowl.

4. Increase to **medium-high** and beat until the beaters leave trails, then on to the stage you want. Move the beaters around the bowl and tilt the bowl so the cream stays pooled under them.

5. Judge by the lift-and-look test (see notes), not the clock. Stop the instant the cream turns matte or grainy. For the most control near stiff peaks, finish the last 15–30 seconds by hand with a whisk. Serve, or cover and refrigerate.

## Notes

- **The three stages — lift the beaters out and read the peak.** *Soft:* the peak flops over immediately; loose and airy — for folding into mousses and batters. *Medium:* the peak stands but its tip curls over; billowy and smooth — the all-purpose stage for topping and dolloping. *Stiff:* the peak stands straight and holds, still glossy — for piping and stabilized fillings. Stop the instant it turns matte or grainy.
- **Watch it near the end.** A hand mixer is forgiving early but still goes from soft to over-whipped in seconds once it's close — soft peaks come at ~2–3 minutes, then only ~30 seconds more to medium and another ~30 to stiff (faster with well-chilled gear at high speed). Don't walk away.
- **Speed and bowl.** A deep bowl gives the two beaters room to circulate the cream; a wide shallow bowl lets it spread thin, drop below the beaters, and splatter. Medium-high — not full speed — gives the best control near the finish.
- **When to add sugar.** Adding it with the cream at the start is simplest; adding it once the cream thickens and the beaters leave trails gives slightly better volume. Confectioners' is the default — it dissolves instantly and the cornstarch in it adds a little stability; granulated works but can leave a slight grittiness. About 14g (2 Tbsp) per 240g cream is lightly sweet; use up to ~28g for dessert-sweet. Salt is optional and divisive; a tiny pinch rounds out the sweetness, more than that reads salty.
- **Cold is everything.** The set is a scaffold of solid butterfat holding air; warm fat turns liquid and collapses the bubbles. Cream straight from the fridge (≤40°F) whips fastest and stiffest — room-temperature cream may never reach stiff peaks. On a hot day, set the bowl in an ice bath.
- **Fat content.** Needs at least ~30% fat to whip; 36% heavy/heavy whipping cream is ideal and the most forgiving. Half-and-half and light cream won't whip.
- **Rescue over-whipped cream.** If it's grainy but not yet broken, whisk in a splash of cold unwhipped cream by hand — 1–2 Tbsp for a slight over-whip, up to ¼ of the original volume if it's worse — to rebuild the emulsion. Once it separates into butter and buttermilk, it's done; start over (or finish making butter).
- **Storage.** Plain whipped cream holds a few hours to about a day before it weeps and deflates; keep it airtight and don't stir it before serving. To hold it longer, whip in a stabilizer: a spoonful of mascarpone or ~½–1 tsp bloomed gelatin per 240g cream (48h–3 days), or confectioners' sugar alone (~12h, the zero-effort option). For weepy leftovers, set the cream in a fine-mesh strainer over a bowl in the fridge — the liquid drains and the cream stays fluffy.
- **Other tools.** *Stand mixer:* same method with the whisk attachment (not the paddle) at medium-high — fastest of the mixers, so easy to over-whip if you walk away. *Immersion (stick) blender:* the fast route — a tall narrow deli container no more than half full, blade fully submerged the whole time (never lift the running head out, or it splatters), medium or pulse, soft peaks in under a minute. *Balloon whisk:* most control, most effort (~5+ minutes). *Food processor or a sealed jar you shake:* both work in a pinch.
- **Scaling.** Cream nearly doubles in volume, so size the bowl up and leave headroom. Every other number scales linearly with the cream weight.
