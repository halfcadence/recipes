# Evaporated & Condensed Milk in Canelés — Flavor Without Wrecking Texture

## The one mental model

Both are just milk with the water boiled off. **Evaporated milk** = whole milk with ~60% of its water removed (≈2x concentrated), sterilized hot enough to pick up a faint cooked/caramel note. **Sweetened condensed milk (SCM)** = the same ~2x concentrate with ~45% sugar dumped in — it ends up **~55% sugar by weight**, so treat it as "milk solids dissolved in syrup," not as milk.

So any swap is: **add milk solids (lactose + protein), subtract water — and for SCM, add a lot of sugar.** Texture only holds if you rebalance the water and sugar you moved. Pin the canelé's two anchors — structure ~12%, total sugar ~23% — and you stay in canelé territory. Blow past them and you get a scorched shell over a center that won't set.

## 1. Composition (per 100 g)

| | Water | Fat | Sugar | Protein |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Whole milk | ~88 | 3.5 | ~5 (lactose) | ~3.3 |
| Heavy cream | ~58 | 36–40 | ~3 | ~2 |
| **Evaporated** | **~74** | **~7.6** | **~10** (lactose) | **~6.8** |
| **Condensed (SCM)** | **~27** | **~8** | **~54–55** (sucrose + lactose) | **~8** |

Evaporated is truly ~1.9–2.0x concentrated — close enough to call "2x" for recipe math. SCM is the outlier: half its mass is sugar.

## 2. What each does to a canelé

**Evaporated → silkier, firmer custard; more browning; subtle cooked-milk flavor.** Swapping it for fresh milk at equal weight removes free water and adds solids: denser, richer, more "set" interior — the same reason flan and tres leches lean on it. More lactose + protein also means the mahogany shell browns *faster*. Honest risk: too much extra protein can make the interior read tight/eggy rather than open and custardy.

**Condensed → sweeter, softer, browns fastest; dulce-de-leche flavor.** The sugar dominates everything. Sugar is hygroscopic and tenderizing — it depresses both starch gelatinization and protein set, so a high-SCM batter sets *softer, wetter, and later*, and browns *faster*. Great for a gooey center, but the failure mode is real: a shell that scorches before a center that may never fully set.

**Flavor honesty.** Evaporated's cooked note is *subtle* — easily buried under rum and vanilla. To actually taste it you need it to be ≥40–50% of the dairy, or all of it. SCM is assertive: 40–80 g reads clearly and synergizes with the caramelized shell.

## 3. The swap rules (the useful part)

Reference batter (~821 g): **340 g milk, 38 g cream, 45 g butter, 170 g sugar, 1 egg + 2 yolks, 85 g AP flour, 15 g cornstarch, 40 g rum.** Roughly 12% structure / 23% sugar / 51% liquid / 10% fat. Hold those.

Component math you'll reuse: 340 g milk = 299 g water + 12 g fat + 17 g lactose + 10 g protein. 38 g cream = 22 g water + 14.4 g fat.

### Evaporated
- **Reconstitute (texture-neutral):** evaporated + equal water ≈ fresh milk. Replace `M` g milk with `M/2` evaporated + `M/2` water. Texture unchanged, only a faint cooked note. A "use up the can" move.
- **Enrich (what you want for flavor):** use evaporated *undiluted* in place of milk. Each 1 g subbed for milk removes ~0.14 g water, adds ~0.04 g protein, ~0.05 g sugar, ~0.04 g fat. Net: more solids, less water, firmer/richer.

### Condensed — the sugar-subtraction rule
Decompose every gram: **1 g SCM ≈ 0.55 g sugar + 0.27 g water + 0.16 g milk solids (~0.08 fat, ~0.08 protein/lactose).**

**Rule: for every gram of SCM you add, remove ~0.55 g granulated sugar.** And watch water — SCM brings only 0.27 g water/g vs 0.88 from milk, so it *dries* the batter. Refloat with plain water if it reads thick.

Worked: **80 g SCM = 44 g sugar + 22 g water + ~6 g fat + ~6 g protein.** So adding 80 g SCM means cutting granulated sugar by 44 g.

## 4. Three swap recipes for the reference canelé

### (a) Light — evaporated for the cream
- 340 g milk · **38 g evaporated (replaces cream)** · 170 g sugar · everything else unchanged.
- You lose cream's 14.4 g fat and gain only ~3 g, so batch fat drops ~11 g. Add back **~11 g butter (→ 56 g)** to hold richness.
- *Analysis:* structure ~12%, sugar ~22.9%, liquid ~51%, fat back to ~9.6%. **Texture essentially unchanged; flavor payoff low.**

### (b) Medium — half the milk → evaporated (recommended)
- **170 g whole milk + 170 g evaporated** · 38 g cream · 170 g sugar · **+45 g water**.
- Swapping half the milk removes ~49 g water and adds ~6 g protein + ~9 g lactose; the 45 g water back restores pourability.
- *Analysis:* structure ~12%, sugar ~23–24% (the +9 g lactose; drop sugar ~9 g if you want it exact), liquid back to ~51%. **Silkier, firmer, clearly cooked-milk flavor.** Pull oven temp ~10°C / shorten the final mahogany phase — it browns faster. Best balance.

### (c) Condensed — dulce/caramel
- Add **80 g SCM**, cut sugar **170 → 126 g**, drop milk **340 → ~320 g** (SCM's low water means little needs displacing), keep 38 g cream.
- *Analysis:* sugar = 126 sucrose + 44 SCM sugar + 17 lactose ≈ **187 g ≈ 22.8%** — on target. Structure ~12%, liquid ~51% (check viscosity; should pour like thin cream). **Softer, moister, gooier interior; deepest, fastest browning.** Counter the soft set: cap SCM at ~80–100 g, hold/bump cornstarch (15 → 18–20 g), and bake ~10–15°C cooler / a touch longer so the center sets before the shell burns.

**Authentic Thai-milk-tea version (b + c combined):** 170 g evaporated + 130 g milk · 60 g SCM · sugar cut to ~135 g · keep 38 g cream. Steep tea in the warm dairy. Cooked-milk body + candied top note, still ~23% sugar / ~51% liquid (top up with water if thick).

## 5. Honest flags

- **Everything browns faster** with these — more lactose, protein, and (SCM) sucrose all feed Maillard and caramelization. Expect to dial oven temp/time down regardless of which swap you pick.
- **SCM's sugar is the texture risk:** overshoot ~23% and you get a soft, wet center under a scorched shell. The sugar-subtraction rule is not optional.
- **Evaporated can over-firm:** too much added protein on top of full eggs reads tight/eggy. Keep it ≤~50% of the dairy unless you also pull back the eggs.
- **Evaporated's flavor is quiet** — you need a meaningful fraction of the dairy (or all of it) to actually taste it.
