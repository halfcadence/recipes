# Combo Pricing — coffee + canelé

A "coffee + canelé" breakfast combo, priced slightly below the sum, to build a morning habit and lift attach + units-per-ticket.

## Thesis (3 sentences)

Both the canelé (~88%) and the coffee (~84% latte, ~86% drip) carry a fat ingredient margin, so a small combo discount comes off a big markup — and if it adds even one unit it would not otherwise have sold, the trade is positive (and a faster sell-through trims the ~10–18% modeled daily waste, a second margin lever). The combo should be a real, visible deal but a shallow one: ~$0.50 off one canelé, ~$1.25 off two, never a deep cut. And it wants the **fast** side of the bar — espresso and batch brew (60–80/hr, seconds to pour) — not the hand pour-over (~4 min/cup), which cannot carry a discounted morning rush (see [plan §5](./plan.md)).

## 1. The unit math

Prices: drip/batch **$3.50**, latte **$5.00**, canelé **$4.25**. Costs ~**$0.50** each (blended canelé ~$0.50 incl. spray; drip ~$0.50, latte ~$0.80 beans+milk). Costs are ingredients only — labor and rent are separate, and labor is the real cost, not these.

| Ticket | Customer price | À la carte | Your cost | Your profit | Eff. discount |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Drip alone | $3.50 | $3.50 | $0.50 | $3.00 | — |
| Canelé alone | $4.25 | $4.25 | $0.50 | $3.75 | — |
| **Drip + 1 canelé** | **$7.25** | $7.75 | $1.00 | **$6.25** | $0.50 (6.5%) |
| **Latte + 1 canelé** | **$8.75** | $9.25 | $1.30 | **$7.45** | $0.50 (5.4%) |
| **Drip + 2 canelés** | **$10.75** | $12.00 | $1.50 | **$9.25** | $1.25 (10.4%) |
| **Latte + 2 canelés** | **$12.25** | $13.50 | $1.80 | **$10.45** | $1.25 (9.3%) |

Even at a real, posted discount the ticket clears **$6–10+ profit on ingredients**. The discount is rounding error against an 85–90% markup; what you are spending is a few dimes to change behavior.

## 2. Tiered structure (recommendation)

- **Coffee + 1 canelé — $0.50 off.** Reads as a clean "breakfast deal," ~5–7% off. Cheap to fund, easy to sign.
- **Coffee + 2 canelés — $1.25 off.** The nudge that earns its keep.

Why push the second canelé. The first canelé in the combo is the attach you wanted anyway; the **second** is near-pure upside. A second canelé adds **$4.25 à la carte for ~$0.50 cost** — even after giving back $0.75 of incremental discount (the $1.25 tier vs the $0.50 tier), the second unit lands ~$3.00 of profit and ~$3.50 of revenue you would not otherwise see. It also moves inventory: the model sells out daily and a faster sell-through cuts the ~10–18% waste tail, so every extra canelé sold early is a unit that does not get marked down or tossed. The 2-canelé tier is the lever; the 1-canelé tier is the on-ramp.

## 3. Breakeven / sensitivity (the honest part)

The combo only pays if it adds units rather than discounting buyers who would have paid full price (cannibalization). Frame it as: how much *incremental* business does the $0.50 give-back need to buy back?

**Per-ticket breakeven.** A $0.50 discount on a drip+1 ticket costs you $0.50 of margin on that ticket. With a canelé netting ~$3.75 of ingredient profit, you recover the discount if the combo converts **~1 extra canelé for every ~7.5 discounted tickets** (0.50 ÷ 3.75 ≈ 0.13 incremental attach) — *but only if every combo sale is genuinely new.*

**The cannibalization correction (the part that's easy to fudge).** That 0.13 figure assumes zero cannibalization. In reality the $0.50 is paid on *every* combo ticket, including buyers who'd have paid full price anyway. If a fraction `c` of combo tickets are cannibalized, the non-cannibalized ones each have to clear `0.13 ÷ (1 − c)` of gross extra attach: **~0.18 at 25% cannibalization, ~0.27 at 50%, ~0.53 at 75%.** So the real bar is closer to **1 extra canelé per ~4 discounted tickets at a plausible 50% cannibalization** — still achievable, but roughly double the headline. Don't quote the 7.5 number without this caveat.

**Attach-rate framing.** Coffee-shop food attach sits around **20–30%** of coffee transactions (multiple worked examples cluster there). *If* the combo lifts attach from ~25% toward the **35–45%** range vendors claim — and *if* that lift is genuinely incremental rather than cannibalized — the added units cover the discount comfortably. Both "if"s are load-bearing: the lift is inference, not a measured benchmark (no clean isolated study quantifies a combo's attach lift for an independent café), and only the non-cannibalized share counts. Treat 35–45% as a planning hope, not a number to bank — and measure your own.

**The real risk is cannibalization.** If the buyer was going to get a coffee *and* a canelé at full price anyway, the combo just handed them $0.50. The discount is "free" only on incremental units. Discipline that limits the damage:

- **Cap at 2 canelés.** No deep multi-unit discounting; the give-back per ticket stays ≤ ~$1.25.
- **Breakfast hours only** (e.g., open–11am). Trains a *morning* habit; keeps the discount off all-day buyers and off the after-work / tourist trade who pay full freight.
- **Fast drinks only — drip/batch and espresso, not hand pour-over.** Operationally necessary (next section) and it naturally fences the deal to the high-velocity rush where added units are most likely incremental.
- **Watch full-price canelé mix.** If à-la-carte canelé sales fall while combo sales rise 1:1, you are discounting buyers you already had — pull the lever back.

## 4. Why it forces the espresso machine

A discounted combo is a **rush** play — it works "especially well during the morning commute," which is exactly when volume spikes. The bar has to clear that volume at the combo price:

- **Espresso + batch carry it.** One group head does **60–80 milk drinks/hr**; batch brew clears most cups in seconds. At ~30–45s of labor per drink, a $5 latte sold in a $8.75 combo is still the fastest-per-labor-minute beverage on the menu — the only one whose labor math beats the **$21.30/hr (loaded ~$26/hr)** wage floor.
- **Hand pour-over cannot.** At ~4 min/cup (~15–20/hr) the pour-over is a ~$6 deliberate, ordered-by-choice premium. Put it in a discounted breakfast combo and you have a slow, low-margin drink stalling the queue at the worst possible hour. Don't.

This is why the combo is a direct argument for the revised [§5](./plan.md) program: the deal is only viable on a bar that can pour fast, and the v1 pour-over-only kiosk could not. The combo needs espresso + batch; the hand pour-over stays the unbundled slow-bar signature.

## 5. Habit / loyalty layer

The point of a thin breakfast discount is **frequency**, not spend per visit — so layer the habit mechanics:

- **Frame it as breakfast, not a discount.** "Coffee + canelé" as a named morning ritual reframes value by perception (bundle price), not by deep cuts. Anchor it next to the premium ($6 hand pour-over, $5 premium canelé) so the combo reads as the obvious value.
- **Punch card.** Loyalty/punch programs are cited to lift purchase frequency ~30–60% and suit exactly the "frequency matters more than spend" daily-habit case (Starbucks is the canonical punch-to-ecosystem example). A physical or simple digital punch card on the combo reinforces the every-morning loop. *(These percentages are vendor-sourced and directional, not RCTs.)*
- **The daily drop is the hook.** The published "today's flavors" sell-out model already creates a reason to come *this* morning; the combo turns "come see today's flavors" into "come get your coffee + canelé." The scarcity and the habit reinforce each other.

## 6. Honest flags

- **The attach numbers are squishy.** The 20–30% baseline and the 10–20% combo-discount depth recur across independent worked examples (medium confidence). The combo's attach *lift* and the loyalty percentages are vendor-blog-sourced, not isolated studies (low confidence). Don't model them as precise; track your own before/after attach and full-price-canelé mix.
- **Discount discipline.** A combo can "train customers to only buy on deal" and cannibalize full-price sales — the documented failure mode (McDonald's $5 meal ran at 1–5% per-bundle margin and only paid off on traffic). Keep the discount shallow, hours-fenced, and capped, and kill it if à-la-carte sales fall 1:1 with combo growth.
- **Don't discount the scarce premium flavors.** The combo should default to the everyday all-stars (chocolate, vanilla) and standard rotating flavors. The premium / limited rotating canelés are scarcity items that sell out on their own — bundling them at a discount throws away pricing power on the exact units that need none. Cap the combo to base-priced canelés; let the premiums stand full freight.
