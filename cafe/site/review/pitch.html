Half Cadence

One pastry, done seriously. A canelé café + slow espresso bar, Seattle.

A half cadence is a musical phrase that stops on the dominant — unresolved, hanging, pulling you toward the next. So is a daily sell-out.

The concept

One product, executed obsessively. The canelé — dark caramelized crust, custardy center, baked at 525°F in copper, batter rested 24–48h.

  • A permanent core for frequency: vanilla, chocolate, Hong Kong milk tea, + a pour-over (coffee) canelé. Regulars order them without looking.
  • A rotating daily drop for the visit: 2–4 flavors, capped counts, a published board, a deliberate sell-out by early afternoon. Flour Box scarcity, run as the brand's heartbeat.
  • A slow espresso bar: single-group espresso (the velocity engine) + hand pour-over (the theater) + batch brew (the rush). The milk-tea and pour-over canelés cross-sell straight into it.

The drop never resolves. You come back tomorrow.

Why it works

  • The math is in the pastry. A canelé costs ~$0.50 in ingredients and sells at $4.25 — roughly 88% ingredient margin, a number no labor-heavy café category touches.
  • The brand is already built and free. An owned two-ink Risograph identity (the canelé is already drawn — pink body, blue dome, overprinting at the seam). Every flavor has a glyph. Packaging is rubber stamps and stickers, not custom print. The daily "today's flavors" board is the marketing — photograph it, post it, done.
  • The owner is the moat. A deep home baker with dozens of tested canelé recipes + a coffee nerd. Brand work is translation, not invention; recurring brand cost is near zero — which matters at a $21.30/hr wage floor.
  • The model has three jobs. The "Today's Six" sampler box (4 all-stars + 2 rotating) is the hero — it packages the rotation as the product and lifts AOV. The breakfast combo (coffee + canelé) builds the daily habit. Espresso is the velocity.

The honest risk

These base-case margins run 3–5× the café benchmark. They hold only if the sell-out is real and the premium price sticks. In a downside case — demand ~40% lower, more waste, lower price — both formats go negative once the owner takes a wage.

Profitability is a demand bet, not a cost-structure win. Validate it before committing capital. This is the whole reason the plan starts small.

The plan

  1. Validate first — no lease. Run a 6–8 weekend farmers-market pop-up in/near Capitol Hill (~$3,900). Cap counts, publish a board, sell out on purpose. Prove people buy at $4.25–4.50 and attach a drink. (WA cottage-food rules make a canelé a TCS food — bake in a commissary under a temporary permit, not a home kitchen.)
  2. Open Lean. ~700 SF 2nd-gen Capitol Hill space, owner bakes, espresso + hand pour-over + batch, 4 all-stars + rotating drop, sell out by early afternoon.
  3. Only then, Full. Scale to the café only if Lean clears break-even with a real owner wage. Do not commit $375k on an unvalidated thesis.

The ask / next step

Fund the validation phase, not the café. ~$4k and a season of weekends answers the four questions that gate everything: is demand real, does the price hold, do drinks attach, is the waste survivable.

Clear those at base-case volume, on a market-realistic price, and the Lean kiosk is the next conversation. Until then, the answer comes from the market, not the model.
