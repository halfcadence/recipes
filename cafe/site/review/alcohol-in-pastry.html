Tea Flavors, Alcohol, and Whether You Can Sell It

Part 1 — Do the tea flavors need the alcohol?

No. In both tea canelés the spirit is purely aromatic, not structural.

How much cooks off. The spirit is stirred into the batter and bakes ~60–66 min (525°F ~11 min, then 375–400°F ~50 min). USDA retention table for stirred-in alcohol: 40% at 15 min, 25% at 1 hr, 10% at 2 hr. So a canelé retains ~20–25%, not zero.

In absolute terms that is tiny:

  • Earl Grey: 30g Cointreau (~12g ethanol) → ~0.2–0.25g ethanol per canelé.
  • Thai tea: ~40g rum (~16g ethanol) → ~0.27–0.33g per canelé.
  • For scale: a 12oz 5% beer is ~14g ethanol. A canelé carries roughly 1/40th of a beer.

Tiny, but not zero — too high to call the item "alcohol-free," far below any intoxicating dose.

What the spirit does. It reinforces a note the tea already supplies. Cointreau echoes the bergamot-orange already in Earl Grey; rum reinforces the vanilla/star-anise/tamarind already in the Thai tea mix. Drop it and you lose nuance, not structure. (HK milk tea is already alcohol-free — nothing to do there.)

Non-alcoholic swaps:

  • Earl Grey (Cointreau →): zest of ~½ orange steeped into the warm milk + 2–4 drops bergamot extract (matches the tea exactly; orange extract is the fallback). Optional whisper of orange-blossom water — 1–2g max, or it reads as soap.
  • Thai tea (rum →): bump the vanilla and add ~2–4g more tea mix; or a few grams of dark molasses/muscovado dissolved in the milk to rebuild the caramel backbone. Skip synthetic imitation-rum flavoring.

Batter-balance fix. The spirit is part of the ~51% liquid fraction. Pulling 30–40g without replacing it thickens the batter and slightly under-crisps the crust. Replace it gram-for-gram with milk: keep the infused milk at 340g and add back the missing 30–40g of milk at the spirit step. Cointreau is ~24% sugar, so an exact match adds back ~7g sugar across the batch — minor enough to ignore. If the crust comes out softer, push stage-two oven +10–15°F or extend 3–5 min.

Recommendation. Make both tea SKUs alcohol-free. You lose almost nothing in flavor and you remove them from every legal question below. Keep the alcohol only where it carries the product — the traditional rum canelé and the chocolate-brandy.

Part 2 — Can you sell pastries made with alcohol?

Bottom line: Yes — no liquor license needed to bake with retail spirits and sell the result in Washington. Confidence: medium.

The legal anchor is RCW 66.04.010, which defines "Liquor" and states it "does not include confections or food products that contain one percent or less of alcohol by weight." A finished canelé at or under 1% ABW is simply not "liquor" in WA, so no WSLCB license attaches to cooking with retail-bought spirits when you sell no beverage. The same definition also keeps a compliant canelé outside the sale-to-minors prohibition (RCW 66.44.270 is written in terms of "liquor"). Federally, TTB regulates alcoholic beverages; the Manufacturer of Nonbeverage Products permit is a tax-drawback mechanism for scale producers buying bulk tax-paid spirits, not a license to bake with a retail bottle. Rum cakes, bourbon balls, and beer bread are sold at markets and retail nationwide without a liquor license.

Caveats (these are load-bearing):

  1. Cottage food vs commercial diverge — and cottage is ambiguous. WAC 16-149-120 lists cakes/pastries/scones as allowable and never mentions alcohol; WAC 16-149-130's prohibited list does not include alcohol — but that prohibited list is expressly non-exhaustive, so silence is not permission. No WSDA source affirmatively blesses alcohol in cottage food. The commercial paths (King County Temporary Food Establishment via commissary, and the future retail café) run on Public Health food-safety code that does not single out alcohol as an ingredient — firmer footing.
  1. The "under 1% ABW" is an estimate, not a measurement. Finished alcohol depends entirely on spirit charge and bake. Published rum cakes range from over 5% ABW down to under 0.5%. A heavily-doused or under-baked canelé could cross 1% and flip into regulated "liquor." Keep the spirit charge small, bake fully (~60 min), and lab-test the rum and chocolate-brandy SKUs before retail scale or if challenged.
  1. Labeling. Cottage-food rules already require a full ingredient list in descending weight order, so rum/brandy/Cointreau must appear regardless. No WA rule mandates a "contains alcohol" callout for sub-1% food, but a voluntary "baked with spirits" note is prudent for pregnant, abstaining, religious, and minor customers.

What to confirm, and with whom:

  • WSDA — written confirmation that alcohol-containing baked goods are eligible under cottage food, before relying on the home-kitchen route.
  • WSLCB — a one-line written confirmation that culinary use under the 1% food exclusion needs no license (advisable for the higher-visibility commissary/retail channels).
  • King County Public Health — the exact TFE / commissary / retail permit steps (food safety, not alcohol).
  • A lab — measured ABW on the rum and chocolate-brandy canelés if you go to retail scale.

So what for Half Cadence

Make Earl Grey and Thai tea alcohol-free — the spirit only echoes the tea, the swaps are clean, and you delete those two SKUs from the entire regulatory question with zero disclosure burden. Keep the traditional rum and chocolate-brandy boozy, since alcohol is the signature there; both sit safely under the 1% food exclusion as long as the charge stays small and they bake fully, with a lab check before retail. And yes — alcohol is a real reason to prefer the commissary path over cottage food: the commercial temp-permit/retail channels are governed by food-safety code that doesn't single out alcohol, whereas the cottage route is genuinely ambiguous on alcohol and you'd be relying on statutory silence. The commissary path turns a "confirm in writing and hope" into a "this is clearly fine."

Confidence: the RCW 66.04.010 "1% ABW food exclusion" is verified verbatim on leg.wa.gov (high). The cottage-food ambiguity (WAC 16-149-120/130, non-exhaustive prohibited list) is verified. The TTB/federal point relies on established knowledge, not a freshly fetched page (medium). The sub-1% ABW result is an estimate, not a measurement — the one number worth lab-confirming before retail. Bake-off cook-loss from USDA Nutrient Data Lab retention tables.
