Flavor Strategy — permanent core + rotating engine

How the board works: a small permanent core you can always find, plus a rotating engine that changes often enough to be the reason to come back. Flour Box-style.

1. The model in three sentences

A canelé shop lives on two opposite jobs. The permanent core is reliability — the 2–4 all-stars a regular orders without looking, the flavors that make "their" canelé a habit. The rotating engine is the scarcity/drop hook — a small set of slots that change on a cadence, announced like a drop, sold until they sell out, and gone.

The board is 4 "slots": the permanent all-stars sit on top, and 2–4 rotating flavors fill the rest of the case each day. Core builds frequency; rotation builds the visit. (Coffee runs alongside as its own program — espresso + batch brew + hand pour-over.)

The Flour Box itself rotates everything — its whole doughnut menu turns over every two weeks, ~4 flavors at a time, Instagram-announced, capped walk-ins until ~12:30 sellout. That's the far end of the dial. We're taking the mechanic (small rotating set, drop energy, sell-out scarcity) and bolting it onto a stable core, the way Crumbl does (6 permanent + 4 weekly rotating) — because canelé regulars want a flavor they can rely on, and a custard is too rich to make people relearn the menu every visit.

Which tea: milk tea over Earl Grey

Both are tested and both are now alcohol-free. Pick Hong Kong milk tea for the permanent slot:

  • Cost. Milk tea is the cheapest flavor we make (~$0.38, ~$0.33 without the optional vanilla bean). Earl Grey is the most expensive (~$0.64) — bergamot leaves are pricey and the recipe historically stacked Cointreau. A permanent flavor bakes daily, so its cost compounds; lead with the cheap one.
  • Breadth. Milk tea (creamy, malty, Ceylon-black, a little caramel) sits next to the vanilla/chocolate/caramel core — it reads as "rich and familiar." Earl Grey's bergamot is floral-citrus and more polarizing; some people love it, some find it perfumey. Polarizing is great in a rotating slot, risky as an everyday anchor.
  • Identity. A milk tea canelé is a clean Seattle hook (boba-adjacent, pan-Asian, photogenic) and a distinctive signature. Earl Grey is more "nice tea flavor."

So: milk tea permanent, Earl Grey rotating (it's a perfect cool-weather rotating tea — keep baking it, just don't anchor on it).

Don't make the core bigger than four. Every permanent slot is a slot the rotation can't use, and the rotation is the reason to come back. Three locks + coffee is plenty; if the 4th feels shaky, run three permanent + an extra rotating slot and let coffee audition in the rotation first.

3. The rotating engine — give the rotation structure

Random isn't a strategy. Give the rotation lanes so each drop feels designed, not thrown together, and so you're always pulling from the owner's strengths. Run 2–4 rotating slots; a good day's pull touches 2–3 different lanes.

Ease shorthand (from the feasibility work): easy = milk-infusion / dry-powder-fold / melt-in chocolate / extract-or-zest (batter chemistry untouched). needs technique = anything wet — route to freeze-dried powder, zest, reduction, or a post-bake fill.

Lane A — Seasonal (fruit + holiday)

The headline lane; this is what makes the board feel alive. Technique is everything here because most seasonal = fruit = water (see §5).

  • Easy: citrus zest (yuzu, blood orange, mandarin, Meyer lemon); spice-led holiday flavors (chai, mulled spice, gingerbread, peppermint, pumpkin spice — the blend, not the purée); eggnog (already tested — lean on the rum + nutmeg); honey-lavender; brown butter; maple.
  • Needs technique: any berry → freeze-dried powder in the batter or a jam/gelée fill after baking; stone fruit → freeze-dried or almond-extract proxy or compote alongside; lemon → zest plus a lemon-curd fill.

Lane B — Tea / Coffee (the owner's strength)

Milk-infusion is the easiest, cheapest, most reliable technique we have, and it's already a tested muscle. This lane should almost never miss.

  • Easy (all infusion or powder-fold): Earl Grey, Thai tea (both tested, alcohol-free), white chocolate matcha (tested), hojicha, jasmine, chai, lapsang (smoky), genmaicha; espresso/coffee, cardamom-coffee, matcha (12g whisked).
  • This is the cheap, low-risk lane to over-index on when you need a safe rotating flavor.

Lane C — Chocolate-plus (riffs on a winner)

Chocolate already sells; the riffs let you rotate without leaving the best-selling flavor family. All melt-in or fold-in — easy.

  • Easy: chocolate-orange (zest), chocolate-mint, mocha/espresso-chocolate, chocolate-mochi (tested), gianduja/hazelnut, salted-caramel chocolate, black-sesame chocolate, chocolate-chili.

Lane D — Wildcard / collab

The "what's that" slot — novelty, savory, local collab, a one-off that earns a photo. Keep it small (one slot) and use the daily-drop to test it cheaply.

  • Sweet-unexpected (mostly easy): black sesame, ube (extract/paste), miso-caramel, tahini-halva, brown-butter-soy, olive-oil-citrus, corn custard.
  • Savory (drop the sugar, lose the caramel shell — that's fine): Gruyère/Comté, rosemary, black pepper-parmesan; bacon or mushroom cooked dry first (needs technique). Garnish a sweet canelé with sharp cheddar for a low-effort sweet-savory play.
  • Collab: a local roaster's beans, a tea house's blend, a jam-maker's gelée fill. No discount — collab flavors are premium and stay full price.

5. Fruit — the honest technique note

Fresh fruit fights the canelé. A canelé is a precise wet custard batter that has to gelatinize into a tender interior and throw a thick dark caramelized shell at high heat. Fresh fruit — or jam, unreduced purée, juice, citrus segments — is mostly water, and that water does three bad things:

  1. Weeps — steams inside the batter, leaving gummy streaks and a soggy, collapsed crumb instead of a clean set.
  2. Dilutes — the gelatinization depends on a tuned liquid ratio; extra free water throws off the egg/starch set.
  3. Kills the shell — surface moisture (and acid) inhibit the deep caramelization, so you get a pale, sticky crust instead of the dark lacquered one.

So the rule is simple: flavor compounds are easy; water is the enemy. Get fruit flavor in without fruit water. The working methods, roughly easiest first:

MethodEaseWhat it's for
Zest (citrus)EasyThe cleanest "fruit" route — oil-soluble aromatics, zero water. Infuse in milk or fold in. Lemon, orange, lime, yuzu, bergamot, grapefruit.
Extract / oil / pasteEasyConcentrated, negligible water. Citrus oils, almond (stone-fruit proxy), banana, berry compounds. Use sparingly.
Freeze-dried fruit powderEasy–medThe best fruit technique. Intense flavor and color, no moisture, won't weep. Raspberry, strawberry, blackberry, blueberry, cherry, passion fruit, mango, banana. Fold into batter or dust the finished shell.
Coconut milk swapEasySwap part of the dairy for coconut milk — a fat-for-fat-ish swap, low risk.
Reduced puréeMed–hardCook fruit down to a thick paste, sub for a fraction of the liquid, re-balance. Finicky (acidity affects set + shell). Treat as an experiment.
Post-bake fill / swirlMediumBake plain, then inject/fill with curd, jam, ganache, or gelée. The wet element never sees the oven. Lemon curd, raspberry jam, passion-fruit ganache.
Compote / coulis alongsideEasy, zero riskThe fruit lives on the plate, not in the pastry. Berry coulis or roasted-stone-fruit compote next to a vanilla canelé.

Never fold in fresh: berries, citrus segments, stone-fruit chunks, apple/pear, melon, grapes — all weep. Best fits: citrus (zest), high-acid berries + passion fruit (powder or fill), coconut (milk swap), stone fruit (powder / almond-extract / compote-alongside).

6. Mapping flavors to the board + boxes

The four-slot board is the box logic. From box-strategy:

  • 4-box = the 4 all-stars (vanilla, chocolate, milk tea, coffee). The "core flight."
  • 6-box = "Today's Six" = 4 all-stars + 2 rotating. The whole menu, today, in one box — the hero/default. Because the 2 rotating slots change, every Today's Six is a different box on a different day: the rotation becomes the product, and the hardest sale (which flavor?) turns into the easiest (all of them).
  • 12-box = 2× each of the six (or 2× all-stars + a wider rotating pull on a 3–4-flavor day). The gift / sharing SKU — not the everyday default.
  • 1 and 2 stay the impulse and combo on-ramp rungs.

This is why the core caps at four: the sampler math (4 all-stars + 2 rotating = 6) only stays clean if the permanent set is exactly four.

Cadence — how often to rotate

  • Core: basically never. Regulars want reliability; that's the point of permanent. Don't reshuffle the all-stars to chase novelty — you'll just annoy the habit buyers who fund your baseline. Re-evaluate the core a couple times a year, not weekly.
  • Rotation: weekly is the sweet spot. Flour Box runs bi-weekly (whole menu), Crumbl weekly (minority of menu, our model). Weekly gives a "come see this week's flavors" hook without churning faster than you can test and bake well. Seasonal themes set the lane; weekly sets the specific flavor; reserve daily specials for the wildcard slot.
  • Use the daily-drop to test cheaply. Daily bake-to-sellout is a free test rig. Bake a small batch of a candidate as a daily special, see if it sells out and gets asked-for. Winners graduate into the weekly rotation; duds cost you one small batch, not a season. This is how you find the next permanent-core candidate without betting the board on it.
  • Announce on Instagram. Every comparable shop reveals the lineup on IG, and the more scarcity-driven the shop, the more the post is the drop. Post the week's rotating slots + the day's specials; let the date/sellout do the scarcity work.

7. Honest flags

  • Don't over-rotate the core. The single biggest risk. The whole value of "permanent" is that a regular never has to think. Rotate the engine all you want; leave vanilla/chocolate/milk-tea/coffee alone. If you find yourself "freshening up" the all-stars, you're eroding the reliability that the rotation's novelty is contrasted against.
  • Fresh fruit will embarrass you if you fold it in raw. Every fruit flavor goes through zest, freeze-dried powder, extract, reduction, or a post-bake fill (§5). A weeping, pale, soggy "strawberry canelé" is worse than no strawberry canelé.
  • Premium / limited flavors stay full price. A rotating wildcard, a collab, a pricier add-in (Earl Grey ~$0.64, matcha) — none of it gets a combo or sampler discount. Scarcity is the pricing power; don't give it away. The box quantity break (box-strategy) is funded out of margin, not stacked on top of limited flavors.
  • Small-batch test before adding to rotation. Especially anything "needs technique" (berries, chestnut, reduced purée, savory). Bake it as a daily special first; only promote to the weekly rotation once it survives a real bake and a real shelf — the shell holds, it doesn't weep, and people actually buy it.
  • Watch the lane balance. A board that's all Lane B (tea/coffee) is safe but sleepy; a board that's all Lane D (wildcard) scares the regular. Aim each window for one seasonal, one safe tea/coffee or chocolate-plus, and at most one wildcard. The easy lanes are the floor; the hard/seasonal flavors are the upside.
