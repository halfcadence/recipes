Box Sizes & Bundles — how big should the default be?

How box sizes and quantity bundles ("1× or 2× of each flavor," by-the-dozen like donuts) move AOV, units, waste, and habit — and whether the hero box should be a dozen.

1. The question, answered up front

Should the default/hero box be a dozen, donut-style? No. Make the hero a 6 — the "today's six" sampler (4 all-stars + 2 rotating). Keep the dozen on the menu as the gift / sharing SKU that anchors the top of the ladder, not the everyday default.

The donut dozen works because a donut is a cheap, shareable, eat-over-two-days commodity: a box of 12 is breakfast-for-the-table or an office drop, and a day-old donut still sells or still gets eaten. A canelé is the opposite — a rich, single-serving custard pastry, best the same day (the dark caramelized shell softens within hours; there is no next-day resale, see packing). Twelve rich custards for one or two people is fatigue and waste, not value. A dozen presented as the default over-asks the everyday buyer and triggers the squishy-eyed "that's a lot" hesitation right at the register.

What the default should do instead: resolve indecision, show off the rotating lineup, and land an honest AOV well above the single — which the 6-sampler does without pushing more rich custard than a person actually finishes.

2. The box ladder

Recommended price ladder. Gentle per-unit quantity break — enough to reward the bigger basket, shallow enough that margin stays fat at every rung. Canelé is $4.25 each at ~$0.50 ingredient cost (canelé-cost); the box/packaging adds ~$0.10–1.00 as it scales.

At the dozen you've given back $0.58/unit (~14%) versus the single, and the box still clears ~84% margin and ~$37 profit on ingredients + box. There is no rung where the discount threatens the model — the quantity break is funded entirely out of a margin that started near 88%. The dozen stays comfortably above 80%. (An elevated ballotin (~$1) is a paid upgrade on the 6 and 12, not the default box.)

3. The sampler mechanic (the good one)

This is the canelé answer to the donut dozen. Tie box size to the 4 permanent all-stars + the day's rotating flavors (flavor-strategy):

  • 4-box = the 4 all-stars. Vanilla, chocolate, milk tea, pour-over — one of each. The "core flight."
  • 6-box = "today's six" = 4 all-stars + 2 rotating. The whole menu, today, in one box.
  • 12-box = 2× each of the six (or 2× all-stars + a wider rotating pull on a 3–4-flavor day). "One to eat, one to share," times six.

Why a variety/sampler fits the rotating-drop model perfectly:

  • It resolves indecision. The hardest moment at a canelé case is choosing among 6–8 flavors. "One of each" deletes the decision — the box is the menu.
  • It sells the rotation. The daily-drop / sell-out model lives on "come see today's flavors." A sampler packages the rotation as the product — every box is a different box on a different day, which turns the scarcity hook into a feature instead of leaving it to fight indecision at the case.
  • It's try-them-all. New customers convert faster on a flight than on a commitment to one $4.25 flavor.
  • It's gift-ready. "One of each" is a complete, legible gift in a way "six chocolate" never is.

A sampler is variety, not volume — it's how you lift units without asking one person to eat a dozen of the same rich thing. That's exactly why it, and not the dozen, is the canelé's answer to the donut box.

4. The hero recommendation

Default / most-promoted box: the 6, branded "today's six." Reasons, in order:

  1. Right size for rich custard. Six is a generous box that two-to-four people finish same-day. It over-asks no one.
  2. It is the rotation. 4 all-stars + 2 rotating means the hero box changes daily — the marketing writes itself off the daily drop, and the box showcases exactly the flavors scarcity is built around.
  3. Honest AOV lift. $23 vs a $4.25 single is a real basket, at ~$3.83/unit — a visible value without a margin dent.
  4. Indecision killer at the case. The single hardest sale (which flavor?) becomes the easiest (all of them).

Around it:

  • Dozen = the gift / sharing / office SKU — promoted around holidays, events, and the ballotin upsell, not as the everyday hero. It anchors the top of the ladder and makes the 6 look like the sensible middle.
  • 4 = "all-stars only" for buyers who want a flight but not the rotating wildcards.
  • 1 and 2 = impulse and the coffee combo. The single is the try-one and the combo on-ramp; the pair is the combo workhorse (combo-pricing).

5. Interaction with waste, sell-out, and the combo

Box ladder and combo do different jobs — don't make one carry the other's load:

  • Bigger baskets pull sell-through forward. A 6 or 12 sold at 9am is six-to-twelve units that don't sit toward a 4pm markdown. Faster sell-through trims the modeled 10–18% waste (§10pct) — the same margin lever the combo leans on. Sampler boxes are a clean way to move the rotating flavors (the ones most at risk on a slow day) because they ride out bundled with the always-popular all-stars.
  • But the dozen is occasion-driven, not daily. Nobody buys 12 rich custards every morning. The dozen's job is the gift / event basket — episodic, high-AOV, planned. Don't expect it to move daily volume.
  • The combo handles the daily habit. Coffee + 1 and coffee + 2 (combo-pricing) own the everyday single/pair and the morning ritual. The box ladder owns the take-home and gift baskets. One builds frequency; the other builds basket size. They don't compete — a customer can run a coffee+1 habit on weekdays and grab a "today's six" on Saturday.

Daily production is oven-bound (~170 lean to ~320 full); sampler boxes that pull the rotating flavors through early are a way to use that ceiling without over-baking any single flavor.

6. Honest flags

  • Dozen-of-rich-custard fatigue is real. This is the core reason not to hero the 12. A canelé is not a donut; the eat-rate-per-person is low and same-day. Over-size the default and you get returns-of-feeling ("too much"), uneaten units, and a value perception that flips to waste. The sampler sidesteps it with variety, not volume.
  • The quantity break can cannibalize the single. Same risk as the combo — a per-unit discount only pays on incremental units. If 6-boxes grow while singles fall 1:1, you've discounted buyers you already had. The break here is shallow ($0.12–0.58/unit) specifically to keep that risk small; watch the single-vs-box mix and pull the per-unit break back if singles erode.
  • Gifting vs self-consumption are different buyers. The dozen's economics assume a gift/share occasion. Market it to solo everyday buyers and it underperforms and trains nobody. Segment the messaging: 6 = "treat yourself / the table today," 12 = "bring it / give it."
  • Box cost rises with size. A single needs almost no packaging; a presentation 6 or 12 (and a ballotin) costs real money and can climb past the ~$0.60–1.00 assumed here. Margins above stay fat anyway, but price the actual box you buy — a fancy dozen box quietly eats the quantity break you intended for the customer.
  • The psychology numbers are squishy. "Sampler resolves indecision," "a 6 is the right size," and the AOV-lift framing are reasoned from the product, not measured. The price points (~$23, ~$44) are round-number anchors, not optimized. Treat the ladder as a starting hypothesis; A/B the hero (6 vs 4), watch attach and same-day sell-through, and let your own register data set the break.
