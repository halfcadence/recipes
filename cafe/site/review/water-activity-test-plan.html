Water-activity test & WSDA determination — chasing the home-kitchen channel

Why this matters

The canelé is presumptively TCS (time/temperature control for safety) — egg/milk/sugar custard batter, moist interior, the kind of food a regulator assumes needs refrigeration. But a food is non-TCS if its finished interior water activity (aw) is ≤ 0.85 (or pH ≤ 4.6, or a safe pH×aw combination). If the moist crumb measures aw ≤ 0.85, the canelé flips to non-TCS and becomes sellable through a Washington Cottage Food Permit — a cheap home-kitchen channel. Per plan.md and farmers-market-playbook.md §0 this is upside, not the primary path: do not redesign the business around it. Spend the smallest dollar amount needed to learn the aw number, then decide.

1) The test

  • Measure: water activity (aw) AND pH, both on the moist interior crumb (not the caramelized shell — the shell reads low and is irrelevant to classification).
  • Why both: classification runs on the FDA Food Code pH×aw interaction table. aw is the deciding variable here (a canelé is near-neutral pH, so pH alone won't save it), but the lab/process authority needs both to place you on the table.
  • Expected result (estimate, not measured): a custardy canelé interior plausibly sits in the 0.85–0.92 band — borderline. Confidence: LOW. This is exactly why you test instead of guess.
  • Outcomes:
  • aw ≤ 0.85 → non-TCS on aw alone. Strongest case. Pursue the listing.
  • aw 0.85–0.92 → likely still TCS; needs a full product assessment / process-authority read of the pH×aw cell. Probably refrigerated-only.
  • aw > 0.92 → TCS. Stop the cottage-food track; commissary/licensed-kitchen only.
  • Get a certified report naming both analytes, suitable for a regulatory / process-authority submission.

3) How to sample & ship a canelé correctly

  1. Bake exactly per your real recipe/process — same molds, time, temperature. The result is valid only for the precise formula and process you submit to WSDA. Any recipe change voids it.
  2. Cool fully to room temperature before sealing. Sealing warm causes condensation that falsely raises aw and skews pH.
  3. Capture the interior. Safest: send whole intact canelés and instruct the lab to test the moist crumb interior (lab controls the cut). Alternative: scoop soft interior crumb only, avoiding the caramelized crust.
  4. Airtight, minimal headspace — food-grade sealed container or vacuum/heat-seal bag, so moisture neither evaporates nor migrates in transit (both shift aw).
  5. Replicates: send 2–3 units from the same batch (labs want replicate readings; aw varies unit-to-unit). Ideally repeat across 2–3 separate batches if WSDA wants proof of process consistency. Label each with batch ID + bake date.
  6. Timing: test the same day or next day — aw drifts as the product stales. If you intend to sell day-2/day-3 product, ask the lab to also test at that intended age so the number reflects real sale conditions. Record elapsed time on the form.
  7. Ship overnight or 2-day courier, rigidly packed (no crushing). Ship cool with a wrapped gel pack in warm weather; do NOT freeze (freezing redistributes crumb moisture). Include: lab submission form, recipe, batch IDs, bake date/time, sample date, and a note requesting aw + pH on the moist interior, certified report.

Validity checklist: same recipe+process as submitted · cooled before sealing · airtight/minimal headspace · interior crumb sampled · tested promptly (and at intended sale age) · replicate units · certified report naming aw and pH.

4) WSDA determination process

The canelé is a "baked good" — the core allowed cottage-food category (WAC 16-149-120) — but it is not individually named on the list, and WSDA states "only products approved by the department and listed in the permit may be produced." So it is not banned; it must be individually approved as non-TCS and written onto your permit.

  1. Contact WSDA FIRST, before applying — and before paying for any lab work. WSDA explicitly says to reach out if you're unsure a product can be approved.
  • Email: cottagefoods@agr.wa.gov (lead with email)
  • Phone: (360) 902-1876 (confirm — one source showed (360) 902-1976)
  1. What to submit in that email:
  • (a) Product description — French canelé; egg/milk/sugar batter baked in molds; intended shelf-stable.
  • (b) Full recipe with ingredient weights / percentages (not volumes).
  • (c) Process steps with times/temperatures — mixing, rest, bake temp/time, cooling, packaging.
  • (d) Intended packaging and a draft label — must carry the WA cottage-food disclosure statement, business name/address, product name, ingredients in descending order, net weight, and allergen declaration (milk, eggs, wheat all apply).
  • (e) Any lab results you already have (finished-interior aw and pH).
  • Ask directly: "Is this approvable as non-TCS, and do you require lab aw/pH data or a process-authority letter?"
  1. How they decide: WSDA evaluates whether the finished food is non-potentially-hazardous against FDA Food Code TCS criteria — non-TCS if finished pH ≤ 4.6 OR aw ≤ 0.85 (or a safe pH×aw combination). Canelé pH is near-neutral, so the deciding number is interior aw. If satisfied it's shelf-stable, they approve and list it on your permit. If borderline, they will likely require certified lab aw/pH data and/or a WSU process-authority letter — or deny it as a TCS product requiring refrigeration.
  2. If green-lit: apply for the Cottage Food Permit, pay the $355 two-year fee, pass a home-kitchen inspection (~6–10 weeks). Sales capped at $35,000/year, direct-to-consumer within WA only.
  3. If they want formal docs: run finished-canelé aw + pH at foodMetrics/MCL (certified report) → optionally send to WSU (Ganjyal, $75) for a process-authority letter → submit the package back to cottagefoods@agr.wa.gov.
Confidence flags: whether WSDA requires lab data at all is not guaranteed — they may approve the canelé as a baked good without testing, or may flag it as a custard/TCS product needing refrigeration. This is precisely why the pre-application email is step one: do not pay for lab work until WSDA tells you they want it. Confirm the current $355 fee, the phone number, and the $35k cap at application time.

Either way, the commissary + temporary-permit path stays the primary route for the market and the storefront — this is purely about whether a bonus home-kitchen channel opens.
