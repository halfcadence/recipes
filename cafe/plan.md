# Half Cadence
### A canelé café and slow pour-over bar, Seattle

*Working name: **Half Cadence** (ties to the owner's existing `halfcadence` recipe site + handle). Alternates retained: Copper & Cream, Demoulé, Forty-Eight.*

---

## Executive summary

**Half Cadence is a small, walkable Seattle café built around one pastry done seriously: the canelé** — dark caramelized crust, custardy center, baked at ~525°F in copper molds, batter rested 24–48h. The model copies what already works in Seattle (Flour Box, Temple Pastries): **four permanent "all-star" flavors** — chocolate, vanilla, milk tea, pour-over (coffee) — plus **2–4 rotating daily flavors**, capped daily quantities, a published "today's flavors" drop, and a deliberate sell-out. Drinks are a **single-group espresso + hand pour-over + batch brew** program (the Poursteady is dropped — see §5), plus a tea program; the milk-tea canelé and the pour-over canelé are direct cross-sells into the bar.

**The design hook:** the whole thing wears an existing, owned **two-ink Risograph identity** (Blue `#0078bf`, Fluorescent Pink `#ff48b0`, overprint `#cc2464`, on cream `#fbfaf8`). The canelé is *already* drawn as a two-shape glyph — a pink rounded-bottom rectangle body capped by a blue circle dome, overprinting at the seam. Every flavor already has its own glyph. Brand work is translation, not invention, so recurring brand cost is near zero — which matters at a $21.30/hr wage floor. The daily flavor board, the box, the cup, and the website are all the same handful of shapes re-typeset.

**Headline numbers (honest version):**

| | Lean kiosk | Full café |
|---|---|---|
| Startup capital | **~$150k** (range $130–185k) | **~$375k** (range $300–520k) |
| Mature monthly revenue (base case) | ~$41k | ~$108k |
| Prime cost (COGS + labor) | ~41% | ~48% |
| Pre-owner-comp operating margin | ~30% | ~30% |
| **Break-even incl. a $6k/mo owner wage** | **~75% of mature sales** | **~66% of mature sales** |
| Months to break-even sales rate | 2–5 | 2–5 |

**The honest flag up front:** these base-case margins are 3–5× the café benchmark (typical café net is 2.5–7%). They hold *only* if the sell-out thesis is real and the premium price sticks. In a downside case (demand ~40% lower, higher waste, lower price) both formats fall to low-single-digit margins and go **negative once the owner takes a living wage**. Profitability here is a bet on demand and pricing power, not on cost structure.

**Recommendation: open Lean, validate demand at a market-realistic price, then decide on Full.** The first move isn't a lease — it's a farmers-market pop-up (see the companion [`farmers-market-playbook.md`](./farmers-market-playbook.md)).

# Part I — Business Plan

## 1. The concept & what makes it defensible

One product, executed obsessively, in a brand and a drinks program nobody else in Seattle is running.

**What's defensible:**

- **Product focus + owned recipe library.** The owner has dozens of tested canelé recipes (custardy, cakey, chocolate, Ferrandi-method, mochi, earl grey, Hong Kong milk tea, Thai tea, matcha). The big-batch recipe is already scaled to a 24-mold tray at 75g/unit (single batch is 70g/unit, 12 from 821g). This is real, not aspirational.
- **A free, distinctive brand.** The two-ink riso system already exists on the recipes site, the canelé is *already* drawn as a two-shape glyph (blue circle dome over pink rectangle body, overprinting to `#cc2464`), and every flavor already has its own illustration. Brand work is translation, not invention — and two-color riso/photocopy collateral plus rubber-stamp packaging keeps recurring brand cost near zero, which matters at a $21.30/hr wage floor.
- **A drink program with no local equivalent.** A hand pour-over / slow-bar signature alongside espresso is differentiated; no surveyed Seattle competitor leads with hand-poured single-origin as theater.
- **Cross-sell hooks.** Milk-tea canelé ↔ tea program; pour-over canelé ↔ coffee program. No competitor has product-drink linkage.

**Honest correction to the original thesis:** the research claimed "0 canelé specialists in Seattle = open white space." That is **overstated**. At least two operators — **Haru** (Korean-French pop-up, canelés as the main offering, including earl grey and matcha) and **Moderne Bakery** ("Kouign Amann & Canelé pop ups in Seattle," preorder/seasonal-flavor model) — already specialize in canelés with a rotating-drop mechanic.¹ The true open space is **a permanent brick-and-mortar canelé specialist with a coffee program** — not the product or the drop format, both of which are already claimed by pop-ups. That changes the pitch from "first mover on a product" to "first to make it a place." We must earn scarcity credibility, not assume it.

## 2. Market & competition

**Why Seattle.** Seattle is one of the strongest US coffee-and-pastry markets — consistently a top-5 / tie-for-first "coffee city" in WalletHub-style studies, coffee-shop density well above the national average, riding a US specialty coffee market estimated at ~$47.8B (2024, ~9.5% CAGR, single-vendor Grand View estimate).² The drop-culture/scarcity model is proven locally (Flour Box, Temple Pastries' preorder cult, General Porpoise).

> ¹ **Correction:** The original research cited "17.4 coffee shops per 100,000 residents" and a clean "#3 WalletHub" rank for Seattle. Verification found 17.4/100k is **San Francisco's** figure (Seattle's density is reported higher), and the WalletHub position is a multi-way tie-for-first in some sub-metrics rather than a clean #3. I state Seattle as "among the highest in the US" rather than cite the mis-attributed number. The "highest per-capita consumption of any US city" superlative is unverified and dropped.

**Competition.** Real, and closer than the brief implied:

## 4. Product & menu strategy

**Structure (the Flour Box model):**

- **Four permanent all-stars:** chocolate, vanilla, milk tea, pour-over (coffee). Reliability anchors.
- **2–4 rotating daily flavors** from the recipe library (earl grey, Thai tea, matcha, chocolate-mochi, eggnog seasonal, savory experiment).
- **Limited daily quantities, published "today's flavors," sell-out as the heartbeat.**

**Pricing.** Market comps cluster at **$3–4/canelé** (Musk Bakery $4.00, Canelé Bakery $3.00, multipacks pencil to ~$3–4). The premium $4.75–5.25 the original model leaned on is a *positioning bet at or above the top of comps*, not a market norm.

> **Honest pricing recommendation:** open at a **defensible premium of $4.00–4.50 single**, test willingness-to-pay, and only push toward $5.00 if sell-outs are consistent. The pro forma below shows both. A $0.50 cut plus weaker attach is most of the gap between base and downside cases — price discipline is doing most of the margin work, so it must be validated, not assumed.

| Item | Open price | Stretch price |
|---|---|---|
| Single canelé (all-star) | $4.25 | $4.75 |
| Single canelé (rotating/premium) | $4.50 | $5.00 |
| Box of 4 (divided 6×6 kraft window box) | $16 | $18 |
| Box of 6 (divided box) | $23 | $26 |
| Box of 12 (12-slot 14×10×4" muffin box) | $44 | $50 |

Box sizing maps directly to the existing packing article: ~60mm footprint per canelé with liner; the 4-cell divided 6×6, the divided 6-box, and the purpose-built 12-slot 14×10×4" box are the SKUs. Each box gets a riso "best eaten today" card — scarcity told honestly, not hidden.

**Track COGS by flavor.** Plain/vanilla ingredient cost is ~$0.39–0.44/unit (recomputed from the 821g recipe; dominated by vanilla bean ~$1.50/batch and aged rum ~$1.00/batch — both volatile, re-quote before finalizing). Chocolate (couverture) and matcha (ceremonial) roughly double that to ~$0.75–1.05. Price premium flavors a touch higher or rotate the expensive ones to manage blended margin.

## 5. Coffee & tea program

**Decision (START — both formats open this way): a single-group commercial espresso machine + manual hand pour-over (V60/Kalita, brewed by hand) + a 1-gallon commercial batch brewer, on a proper café water filter. The ~$12k Poursteady is dropped entirely.** Espresso is the velocity-and-margin engine, hand pour-over is the slow-bar signature/theater, batch brew is the fast default. Drinks remain ~50% of modeled revenue; the milk-tea and pour-over canelés cross-sell straight into the bar.

**This corrects the v1 plan.** v1 opened the Lean kiosk pour-over-*only* on a ~$12k Poursteady and deferred espresso to Full. That was the wrong trade, for three reasons:

- **Espresso is the morning-commuter margin and velocity engine — v1 admitted it was forfeiting it.** A single group head does **60–80 milk drinks/hr**; a hand pour-over is ~3–4 min/cup (~15–20 cups/hr). At a **$21.30/hr no-tip-credit wage floor**, the espresso drink is simultaneously the highest-ticket *and* the fastest-per-labor-minute beverage — the only one whose labor math beats the wage. Pour-over-only surrendered the entire 7–9am commuter segment and, with it, the bulk of typical café beverage revenue.
- **Hand pour-over is cheap and *more* on-brand than a robot.** A barista hand-pouring a V60 on a lit bar *performs* the "slow bar" promise; a $12k automated Poursteady hides it inside a machine. Manual gear (drippers, a gooseneck kettle, a brewing scale, servers) is **a few hundred dollars, not $12k.**
- **Batch brew carries the rush.** A 1-gallon satellite brewer clears most cups in seconds, so neither espresso nor the hand pour-over ever bottlenecks the queue.

Decouple ORDER → BREW → PICKUP so a 3–4 min hand pour never stalls the line; the hand pour-over becomes a deliberate ~$6 single-origin ordered *by choice*, not the rush default.

### Net capex delta vs the ~$150k Lean budget

The v1 Lean beverage capex was **Poursteady $12,000 + "EK43 grinder + Fetco batch brewer + filtration" $6,500 = ~$18,500 expected.** The revised espresso-led package is **~$10,665 expected** — about **$7.5k *less*, before contingency.** Net: the Lean total stays **~$143–150k while *gaining* espresso.** Espresso is added by deleting the single most overpriced v1 line (the Poursteady), not by spending more.

> **Net effect: a capability upgrade at a ~$7.5k saving.** This is the rare change that adds the morning-commuter revenue lever *and* reduces capex.

### Revenue / throughput rationale

Espresso converts the program from a slow-only purist bet into a real café mix and fixes v1's two admitted weaknesses (forfeited morning commuter + queue-stall risk):

- **Attach.** v1 modeled ~0.8–1.0 drinks/customer pour-over-only. Espresso captures the latte/cortado/Americano commuter who won't wait 4 min, lifting attach toward ~1.0–1.2 and converting pastry-only walk-bys.
- **AOV + labor.** A $5.50+ milk drink beats batch brew (~$3.50–4) at ~30–45s of labor vs 3–4 min for a hand pour — the high-ticket drink is now also the *fast* drink, the inverse of the v1 problem.
- **Rush throughput.** One group does 60–80 milk drinks/hr vs hand pour-over's ~15–20 cups/hr. Espresso + batch brew clear the 7–9am crowd while hand pour-over becomes the deliberate ~$6 premium/theater choice.

## 6. Operations & production

**Oven capacity → daily ceiling (the binding constraint).** The real limiter is **heat recovery**, not the dial — a full tray of cold copper molds is a big heat sink, so a full tray starts ~25°F hotter (550°F) on a preheated baking steel. Tested cycle: 525°F shock 11 min → 400°F for 50–55 min ≈ ~65 min bake + ~10 min load/unmold/reset = **~75 min per oven turn**, 24 canelés/tray.

| Turns over baking window | Daily canelé ceiling (single oven) |
|---|---|
| 1 tray/turn, ~12h window | ~230/day |
| 2 trays/turn | ~460/day |
| 3 trays/turn | ~690/day |

> These are computed from cycle time and assume the oven recovers heat fast enough for multiple trays per turn. **Real convection heat-recovery (the owner's flagged true limiter) could cut this materially — bench-test on the actual oven before committing mold counts and volume.** Lean needs ~170/day, Full ~320/day — both within reach, but Full leaves little slack for a second drop or a failed batch.

**The 24–48h batter rest — a planning asset, not a cost.** Batter keeps 4–5 days refrigerated (tested), so mixing is decoupled from baking: **mix on a 2-day-ahead rolling schedule, bake trays to forecast through the day.** This turns the rest from a bottleneck into a steady cadence — and into brand storytelling ("Rest 48 hours. Bake at 525. Eat today."). Size refrigeration for ~2 days of batter *plus* dairy/ingredients; under-sizing the fridge is a common, costly mistake.

**Shelf-life / waste discipline (the #1 margin lever).** Canelés are best within hours; the recipe says "Cool 2 hours. Eat the same day" — effectively zero next-day resale. The model assumes 12–18% waste. The drop/sell-out culture, pre-orders, and a live per-flavor sell-out board push waste toward 10%. **Each 5 points of waste reduction ≈ ~1 point of net margin.** Over-production on a slow day is a direct margin leak; the same scarcity that markets the brand also caps revenue.

**Molds.** Size to **peak simultaneous oven capacity × bakes-before-first-demold**, NOT daily volume — canelés are demolded shortly after baking and reused same-day. ~250 molds supports a few-hundred/day operation with fast re-coat between bakes. Beeswax is optional; tested nonstick spray works fine and is faster prep. Note copper molds are **$34–56 each** at restaurant supply (Matfer/de Buyer), not the prosumer $15–30, so ~250 molds is a real ~$11k line, not a few thousand — see §7.

**Staffing & hours.** Wage floor is **$21.30/hr (2026, flat citywide rate, no tip credit, tips 100% additive; the small-employer tier was eliminated Jan 1 2025)**; budget loaded labor at ~$26–27/hr. Hours: 7am–2pm-ish, front-loaded to the morning drop, **sell out by early afternoon**.
- **Lean:** owner bakes + works the bar; ~1 PT barista. One person must run POS + pull espresso + batch brew off-peak; reserve hand pour-over for slower periods or as a quoted-wait premium, never the only rush option (see §5). Espresso adds a daily-maintenance + second-skill burden the v1 pour-over-only plan dodged — budget a small annual machine-service line.
- **Full:** owner + 1 baker/lead + 2–3 baristas covering 6–7 days.

| **Monthly revenue** | **~$41,000** (~$492k/yr ≈ ~$703/SF — *aggressive*, labeled so) |

### Full café (~1,200 SF, small team, +espresso, 7 days)

| Metric | Base case |
|---|---|
| Volume | ~320 canelé + ~340 drinks/day |
| Daily revenue | ~$3,550 |
| **Monthly revenue** | **~$108,000** (~$1.29M/yr ≈ ~$1,076/SF — *top-decile, optimistic*) |
| Prime cost | **~48%** |
| Pre-owner-comp operating profit | ~$35,000/mo (**~32%**) |
| Net after $4,500/mo owner wage | ~$30,000/mo (~28%) |
| **Break-even** | **~59% of mature sales** at $0 draw; **~66% (~$71k/mo)** incl. $6k owner wage |
| Owner draw sustainable at base | $6,000–8,000+/mo |

> **Two honesty flags carried from verification:**
> 1. **Full's $3,550/day quietly requires the top of every range at once** — $5.25/canelé *and* ~$1,050/SF (Starbucks-tier retail productivity) *and* 7-day sell-out, in a space that includes non-selling seating. Treat Full base-case revenue as a **stretch goal, not a forecast.**
> 2. **The downside case is the one to plan against.** Demand ~40% lower + higher waste + lower price → **Lean ~$24k/mo rev, ~6% pre-comp margin, NEGATIVE (-~$3k/mo) after an owner wage; Full ~$62k/mo, ~2% pre-comp, NEGATIVE after an owner wage.** Base-case margins of 23–34% are 3–5× the 2.5–7% café benchmark — plausible *only* under premium price + sub-15% pastry COGS + sell-out. Profitability is a bet on demand and pricing power.

**Therefore: start Lean.** Lower fixed base, break-even at ~75% of mature sales including a living owner wage, and a far more forgiving downside than Full's larger labor + rent load.

### The fluted-canelé form as system motif

Abstract the canelé into a **token, not a drawing**, so it scales and recombines:

- **Body** = pink rounded-bottom rectangle.
- **Dome** = blue circle.
- **Flutes** (optional, large scale only) = thin vertical blue rules over the pink body, overprinting to `#cc2464` — the only time flutes appear, and only on window-sized graphics where they read.

### Color discipline

Two inks + the one overprint, full stop. Cream `#fbfaf8` is the only ground. Write the rule down so seasonal pressure can't erode it:

> **Permanent palette:** `#0078bf`, `#ff48b0`, overprint `#cc2464`, ground `#fbfaf8`. A third ink is allowed **only** as a rare, dated, single-drop seasonal exception that never enters the permanent system. The overprint covers the day-to-day need for a "third color."

Two practical specs to protect it: (1) lock `#0078bf` and `#ff48b0` to **Pantone/spot equivalents** and proof on actual cream stock — Fluorescent Pink drifts toward muddy magenta in 4-color digital, backlit signage, and on screen; vendors *will* get it wrong unless specified. - Honesty as tone: `Best within hours. We sell out. Come early.`
- Drinks: `Pour over. ~4 minutes a cup. Worth it.`

## 13. The rotating-flavor mechanic as a brand ritual

The daily flavor drop *is* the brand's heartbeat and its primary social object. It maps directly onto the recipe site's per-recipe illustration grid: each flavor already is — or trivially becomes — a **2-shape riso glyph**, so the daily board is just a re-typeset of that grid.

### The "Today" poster

An **A2 sheet on cream stock, re-run every morning** (riso, photocopier, or in-house printer — cheap by design). Structure:

```
                          COPPER & CREAM
                          [canelé glyph]
        ──────────────────────────────────────────────
        TODAY            wed 25 jun        bake 7:30
        ──────────────────────────────────────────────
        ALL-STARS   (always here)
        [▮●] chocolate     [●▮] vanilla
        [▮▬] milk tea      [▲●] pour over
        ──────────────────────────────────────────────
        TODAY ONLY
        [glyph] earl grey      ● ● ● ● ● ○ ○ ○
        [glyph] thai tea       ● ● ● ○ ○ ○ ○ ○
        [glyph] eggnog         SOLD OUT  (struck through, pink)
        [glyph] white choc matcha  ● ● ● ● ● ● ● ●
        ──────────────────────────────────────────────
        Best within hours. We sell out. Come early.
```

- **Fixed top band** = the four permanent all-stars, in a constant order and position so regulars find them instantly:
  - **chocolate** — `chocolate-canele.svg`: blue rectangle body + pink circle dome (the *inverse* of the master glyph).
  - **vanilla** — the master glyph itself: pink rectangle + blue circle.
  - **milk tea** — `hong-kong-milk-tea-canele.svg`: pink rectangle + blue base rectangle.
  - **pour over** (coffee) — pour-over triangle + a small canelé dot, riffing on `iced-pour-over.svg` (blue triangle + pink circle).
- **Rotating band below** = today's roster, each as its existing 2-shape glyph beside a monospace name and a **dot grid** showing remaining count.
- **Sold-out ritual:** staff knock down dots as counts deplete; at zero, the name gets a **pink overprint strike-through** and the card flips to a `SOLD OUT` state. The strike *is* the scarcity signal — legible from the door, honest, and photogenic.

### The ritual loop

Photograph the fresh poster every morning → it's the day's Instagram post (see §16). The poster is simultaneously the menu, the wayfinding, the marketing object, and the operational truth (it matches the live web/app state). Drawing a new flavor costs one SVG: max 2 primary shapes, blue/pink only, viewBox `0 0 200 200`, per the existing illustration rules — so the roster can grow indefinitely at near-zero design cost.

**Guardrail:** the drop must be *consistent enough* that "we sell out" reads as craft, not scarcity theater. Forecast daily; if all-stars routinely vanish before the mid-morning crowd, raise their counts. The rotating flavors are where scarcity is allowed to bite. (This is the same waste/forecast discipline as §6 — the board is the operational truth, not just decoration.)

## 15. Service & customer journey

An 8-step single forward funnel:

1. **Walk-by.** Corner glazing + riso window mark + bake smell venting *near* (not into) the entry. The filled window bar is live social proof.
2. **Drawn in.** Door opens toward the case; the case is the first thing seen.
3. **Read today's flavors.** Board is read on approach, not at the register. Decisions are made before the POS.
4. **Queue along the case.** Line runs parallel to the display — waiting = browsing. Intentional queue management.
5. **Order + pay.** Single POS. Barista **quotes a realistic pour-over wait** and hands a numbered riso token (or sends an SMS).
6. **Watch the bar.** Customer steps to the bar sightline; the barista's hand pour-over (or the espresso pull) is the show. Decoupled from the register so the next person orders while this cup brews.
7. **Pickup.** Cup and/or pre-boxed canelés at a separate handoff point.
8. **Go or sit.** Fork to the door (dominant grab-and-go) or the window bar / two-tops (dine-in minority).

### Queue management with a slow bar

The central operational risk is that a 3–4 min pour-over stalls the morning rush. Mitigations, all structural:

- **Physically decouple ORDER (D) → BREW (E) → PICKUP (F).** No single station blocks another.
- **Espresso + batch brew are the fast defaults** for rush and clear in seconds-to-a-minute; the **hand pour-over is the premium / theater option** (~$6, ~4 min), a choice not a bottleneck.
- **Order-ahead** (web/app tied to the morning drop) plus **numbered riso tokens** for in-store orders removes queue before customers arrive.
- **Single-staff-able off-peak:** layout lets one person run POS + espresso + batch during slow hours, important at **$21.30/hr** (verified 2026 Seattle minimum, flat citywide rate, effective Jan 1 2026 — the small-employer tier was eliminated Jan 1 2025).
- **Size Zone E for espresso + hand pour-over + batch from day one.** Per §5, both formats open with all three; don't design a bar that can't seat the espresso machine + grinder + a pour-over station + filtration plumbing.

### Graceful sell-out signaling

- The board's **per-flavor dot grid** depletes through the day; sold-out flavors get a **pink overprint strike** and a flipped `SOLD OUT` card.
- **Mirror it live** on the web/app today's-flavors page so walk-bys and order-ahead users see the same truth — nobody is disappointed at the register.
- Copy carries the contract honestly: `Best within hours. We sell out. Come early.`

### ADA / accessibility

- **36" clear circulation** entry → funnel → pickup; the queue-along-the-case must not pinch below 36" at rush.
- **Lowered ~34" POS section**; at least one ~28–34" knee-clearance window-bar seat.
- Corner door meets clear-width and maneuvering-clearance rules.
- **High-contrast type on cream:** primary copy and prices in **blue or overprint-dark, never pink alone** (pink-on-cream fails contrast). Board type large enough to read from the door.
- Confirm seat count against the lease's **occupancy + restroom** requirements — these can force or cap dine-in (and they drive the Full-format ADA restroom line in §7).

# Gaps & Next Experiments

Before signing a lease, the plan's profitability is a **demand-and-pricing bet**, not a cost-structure win. The base case is 3–5× the café benchmark and holds only under premium price + sub-15% pastry COGS + a real sell-out. These are the most important unknowns and the cheap ways to de-risk each one *first*. Every test below costs hundreds to low-thousands of dollars, not the $150k–$375k a lease commits.

| # | Unknown (the question) | Cheap experiment to de-risk it | What "go" looks like |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Real daily demand at a market-realistic price.** The single load-bearing assumption. Lean needs ~170 canelé + ~140 drinks/day; is that real in the target neighborhood? | **Run a 4–8 week farmers-market stall or weekend pop-up** in/near Capitol Hill. Cap quantities, publish a "today's flavors" board, sell out on purpose. Count units, attach rate, and time-to-sell-out. | Consistent sell-through near the modeled volume *without* dropping price; sell-outs land mid-morning, not at open. |
| **2** | **Willingness-to-pay: $4.00–4.50 vs $5.00.** A $0.50 cut + weaker attach is most of the gap between base and downside. | **A/B price at the stall** across weeks (or two simultaneous markets). Track conversion and units at each price; watch where the line balks. | $4.25–4.50 clears at modeled volume; $5.00 holds only on rotating/premium flavors without killing conversion. |
| **3** | **Drink attach rate + the bar mix.** Drinks are ~50% of modeled revenue. The plan now opens with espresso + hand pour-over + batch — does the mix attach and clear the rush? | At the pop-up, run **hand pour-over + batch** (espresso is hard to haul to a market) and measure drink attach + rush balk. The market under-tests espresso, so treat attach there as a floor; confirm espresso throughput in the buildout. | Drink attach near the modeled ~0.8–1.0; hand-pour rush balk low enough that batch + (in-store) espresso will clearly carry peak. |
| **4** | **Real waste %.** Modeled at 12–18%; 20–25% kills the margin advantage (each 5 pts ≈ ~1 pt net margin). | **Bake-to-forecast at the pop-up** with a pre-order list and a live sell-out board. Log every unsold canelé for 4–8 weeks. | Waste trends toward ≤12% once the pre-order + drop discipline is running. |
| **5** | **Oven heat-recovery → true daily ceiling.** Cycle-time math says ~230/turn-window per tray, but real convection recovery (the owner's flagged limiter) may cut it. This sets mold count and volume cap. | **Bench-test the actual oven** (or the exact model) with full cold copper trays back-to-back. Measure real trays/turn and recovery time. | Confirmed trays/turn supports Lean ~170/day with slack for a second drop or a failed batch. |
| **6** | **Pre-order conversion — does it actually pull queue out of the store?** The `/today` + reserve-a-box lever is what makes a slow bar survive a rush. | **Stand up a one-page `/today` + reserve-a-box form** for the pop-up (even a simple form). Measure what share of sales come pre-ordered vs walk-up. | A meaningful pre-order share (e.g. 20–40%) that smooths the rush and validates the digital-twin loop. |
| **7** | **True occupancy cost + 2nd-gen availability.** Asking rents are not effective rents; NNN/CAM is a $8–15/SF estimate; the 2nd-gen ex-food space is the single highest-leverage capex decision. | **Tour 5–10 real ~700–1,000 SF listings**, get written NNN/CAM per LOI and a GC walk-through bid on one promising 2nd-gen space. No commitment. | A real 2nd-gen space with inherited hood/grease at all-in ≤ ~$50/SF and a buildout bid inside the §7 Lean range. |
| **8** | **Brand-on-substrate: does the two-ink riso survive the real world?** Fluorescent Pink drifts to muddy magenta in print, signage, and on screen; the whole identity rests on it landing. | **Print the poster, box card, cup stamp, and a vinyl swatch** on actual cream stock at counter distance, plus a backlit-sign proof. Confirm Pantone/spot equivalents and the door-distance legibility of the sold-out strike. | `#0078bf` / `#ff48b0` / `#cc2464` reproduce faithfully on cream across paper, vinyl, and backlit; the board reads from the door. |

**Sequencing.** Items 1–4 ride on the *same* farmers-market / pop-up — one experiment answers four of the five biggest unknowns at once, for the price of a stall fee, ingredients, and a stack of riso posters. Run that first. Items 5–8 are parallel desk/bench work. **Do not sign a lease until items 1–4 clear at base-case volume on a market-realistic price — and even then, open Lean, not Full.**
